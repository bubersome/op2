# Huobi’s New Boss Shakes Up Crypto Firm With China Plan

## Justin Sun’s strategy for boosting revenue includes a novel way around Beijing’s trading restrictions

![img](https://images.wsj.net/im-734721/?width=1278&size=1)

Cryptocurrency entrepreneur Justin Sun is attempting to revive the fortunes of digital-assets exchange Huobi by shifting its focus back to China—with the aid of a digital citizenship program from a tiny Caribbean island.

Huobi, founded in 2013, was one of the world’s biggest crypto exchanges before China’s crackdown, boasting around 19% of global crypto trading volumes in 2020, according to CoinGecko, a data provider. In 2022, after it closed the accounts of its clients in China, its market share fell to 4%. 

Other exchanges based in China closed or made a successful shift to other markets. The world’s largest crypto exchange, Binance, which was founded in China in 2017, quickly moved out of the country as regulatory pressure increased. But Huobi struggled to adjust its business model after the crackdown in China—and Mr. Sun now wants the company to turn its attention back to the country.

Huobi has no executives with “C-suite” titles—such as chief executive officer or chief financial officer—and it describes Mr. Sun, 32, as an adviser, part of a team that provides strategic guidance to the company. But Mr. Sun has effectively functioned as the company’s leader since late last year, according to people familiar with the matter. 

His plan to increase Huobi’s revenue includes a novel way around China’s crypto trading restrictions, according to some of the people. Huobi has signed a partnership with the Commonwealth of Dominica, a small island nation that is willing to grant so-called digital citizenship, even to those who have never set foot on its shores. 

On Huobi’s app, new users who choose Chinese as their nationality while verifying their personal details are prompted to apply for Dominican digital citizenship. People familiar with the program said Chinese users will be able to trade on Huobi’s platform if they register for Dominican digital citizenship. Customers from other countries can also apply, but Huobi doesn’t automatically direct them to do so.

A spokesperson for Huobi said the company “uses KYC verification to prevent China users from doing such things,” referring to a “know your customer” process that is needed to check the identity of clients. But two Chinese nationals in the country said they had used the Dominican digital identity to trade on the platform.

The collaboration with Dominica, which has a population of around 72,500, is “the first significant step in Huobi’s strategy of global development,” the company said in November. Huobi started accepting applications for the program the following month. 

“The Caribbean strategy is definitely one of the most important priorities for Huobi,” Mr. Sun said in an interview. “We want to advocate this kind of freedom of nationality concept.”

Ben Charoenwong, an assistant professor in finance at the National University of Singapore, said the Dominican digital citizenship program could help Chinese nationals who want to trade cryptocurrencies evade the reach of regulators in the country.

“It’s easier when you don’t show up as a Chinese citizen; you’re out of the Chinese financial system,” he said.

Some people in China have been able to get around the crypto trading restrictions by accessing exchanges through virtual private networks, which mask their real location, and trading in the peer-to-peer market. Huobi’s push to win business from the country goes a step further than other exchanges in giving Chinese customers a way to trade.

Mr. Sun has been clear about the company’s intent to attract Chinese customers. He called Huobi [“the” exchange for China](https://twitter.com/justinsuntron/status/1627721354685591553) in a tweet and said on Feb. 20 that the exchange had listed TCNH, a stablecoin pegged one-to-one to the offshore yuan, the more freely-traded version of China’s currency. The stablecoin, which runs on Tron, a blockchain company founded by Mr. Sun, has a circulation of about 10 million as of March. 

Huobi is taking a more conventional approach in Hong Kong, which is working hard to [lure back digital-asset companies](https://www.wsj.com/livecoverage/stock-market-news-today-02-14-2023/card/financial-firms-want-a-piece-of-hong-kong-s-crypto-pie-BvCmvE9YYXfGkqWHF1cP?mod=article_inline) after its reputation as a business hub suffered under [its strict Covid-19 policies](https://www.wsj.com/articles/hong-kong-lifts-citywide-mask-mandate-after-almost-three-years-da759c3a?mod=article_inline). The exchange plans to set up its Asia headquarters in Hong Kong, Mr. Sun said in the interview. It is applying for a license in the city and intends to build its team there within the next six months, he said. Huobi is registered in Seychelles, has an office in Singapore and is based in the Caribbean, a company spokesperson said.

Mr. Sun said that he sees Hong Kong as an “experimental zone” for the rest of China, and that he thinks the city—governed under an arrangement with China known as “one country, two systems”—wouldn’t have been able to push for its crypto-friendly policies without approval from the central government in Beijing. 

“If we can really prove ourselves in Hong Kong, I think the crypto future for Asia is also very big, very promising,” he said. 

China’s central bank said in response to questions that its attitude toward cryptocurrency-related activities hadn’t changed, and that overseas exchanges are strictly prohibited from providing trading and other services to people in the country. The People’s Bank of China said it would “promptly deal with domestic and foreign trading platforms that provide virtual currency-related services to residents in China.”

U.S. authorities have intensified a crackdown on the crypto industry. The Securities and Exchange Commission said on March 22 that it [had filed civil charges](https://www.wsj.com/articles/crypto-promoter-justin-sun-draws-the-spotlight-this-time-from-regulators-d45b848?mod=article_inline) against Mr. Sun and three of his companies alleging they had offered two unregistered crypto tokens and manipulated the prices of one of them. Mr. Sun tweeted that the SEC’s complaint “lacks merit.” When asked for comment, a spokesperson for Huobi referred The Wall Street Journal to Mr. Sun’s tweet.

Confidence in Huobi has recently been tested, with [its native token HT](https://www.wsj.com/livecoverage/stock-market-news-today-03-10-2023/card/huobi-s-crypto-token-slides-firm-plans-liquidity-fund-nWLHroCsLPz1yPFEzyxZ?mod=article_inline)momentarily plunging earlier this month and falling again after charges were made against Mr. Sun.

Huobi’s shift in strategy has been accompanied by a series of aggressive cost cuts, in line with the wider crypto industry. Huobi said in January it [planned to lay off around a fifth of its staff](https://www.wsj.com/livecoverage/stock-market-news-today-01-06-2023-december-jobs-report-unemployment-rate/card/crypto-exchange-huobi-to-lay-off-20-of-staff-5UvqEw5TKJnZdkJvT8Kp?mod=article_inline), a move that Mr. Sun said was intended to help the company “prepare for a longer bear market.” Huobi employed at least 1,600 people around the time Mr. Sun took charge, a number that had fallen to roughly 1,200 by the middle of January, according to people familiar with the matter. The cuts are still being made. 

In December and January, Huobi told its staff in mainland China, through video calls and in-person meetings, that full-time staff would be moved to freelance consultant contracts that amount to lower pay and that their insurance and social security would be cut, according to people with knowledge of the terms. The company denies doing that.

Hong Kong-based investment firm About Capital Management (H.K.) Co. took over Huobi from founder Leon Li, a former coder at 

[Oracle](https://www.wsj.com/market-data/quotes/ORCL)

Corp., in October. The acquisition’s terms weren’t disclosed. Mr. Li and About Capital didn’t respond to requests for comment.

Mr. Sun became known for paying $4.6 million in a charity auction [to dine with billionaire investor Warren Buffett](https://www.wsj.com/articles/crypto-promoter-wins-bidding-war-for-lunch-with-warren-buffett-11559587135?mod=article_inline). He was born in the western Chinese province of Qinghai, and studied at Beijing’s Peking University before getting a master’s from the University of Pennsylvania. He was named ambassador and permanent representative of Grenada to the World Trade Organization in December 2021. He often uses the title “His Excellency,” or H.E., when referring to himself, and his website describes him as a Chinese-Grenadian diplomat.

On Jan. 4, Mr. Sun addressed the entire company on a chat group, attempting to calm frustrations over the cost-cutting drive. “We will be able to increase the exchange’s profitability and operational effectiveness by cutting the fat,” he said, according to messages seen by The Wall Street Journal. 

“This is my 87th day since joining Huobi,” he wrote in a lengthy text in Chinese. “During these three months, I and the new management team have been doing our best. In the last 87 days, we have worked past 2 a.m. every day, hoping to gain recognition from all Huobi users and employees.”

Mr. Sun signed the message “His Excellency, Sun Yuchen,” using his Chinese name.
