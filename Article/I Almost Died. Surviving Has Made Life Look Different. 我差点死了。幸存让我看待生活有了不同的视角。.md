# I Almost Died. Surviving Has Made Life Look Different. 我差点死了。幸存让我看待生活有了不同的视角。

## I now understand why emerging from a near-death experience turns some people insane. 我现在明白为什么从濒死经历中挣脱出来会让一些人变得疯狂。

May 17, 2024 at 9:46 pm

![img](https://images.wsj.net/im-960783/?width=1278&size=1)

Several years ago, at age 58, I was saying something to my wife when I felt a strange pain shoot through my lower abdomen. It wasn’t unbearable, but it wasn’t pleasant either, so I stood up to see if that would make it go away. The floor reeled away from me as if I was on the deck of a ship, and I sat back down. We were in a cabin in the woods a short walk from our driveway, but that distance suddenly seemed insurmountable. My wife, Barbara, helped me stumble down the trail until we got to our car, and then she put me into the passenger seat and called 911. By then the sky had turned electric white, and I was going blind.
几年前，我 58 岁那年，我正在和妻子说话时，突然感到一阵奇怪的疼痛穿过我的下腹部。这种疼痛并不难以忍受，但也不舒服，于是我站起来看看是否会消失。地板像我站在船甲板上一样晃动，我又坐了下来。我们当时在离我们车道不远的树林小屋里，但那段距离突然间显得不可逾越。我的妻子芭芭拉帮助我蹒跚走下小径，直到我们到达车边，然后她把我放在副驾驶座位上，打电话叫了 911。那时天空已经变成了电白色，我开始失明。

The hospital was an hour away by ambulance, and by some miracle I was still alive when we got there. By then my blood pressure was 60 over 40, and I was drifting in and out of consciousness. As a doctor tried to insert a large-gauge needle into my jugular, a deep, dark pit opened up beneath me and my dead father appeared overhead to reassure me. I was horrified and said to the doctor, “You got to hurry, you’re losing me right now.” That was the last thing I remembered for quite a while.
医院离这里一个小时的救护车路程，但奇迹般地，我们到达时我还活着。那时我的血压是 60/40，我时而清醒时而昏迷。一名医生试图在我的颈静脉插入一根粗针时，我感觉自己仿佛掉进了一个深邃黑暗的深坑，我已故的父亲出现在头顶上安慰我。我感到恐惧，对医生说：“你必须快点，你现在正在失去我。”那是我记得的最后一件事，有一段时间我什么都不记得了。

Almost dying and then returning to the world of the living is not the relief one might expect. Barbara and I have two little girls, aged six months and three years at the time, and seeing them waiting excitedly with their babysitters as I came up the dirt drive racked me with sobs. Instead of elation, I found myself beset with a terrible and irrational fear that maybe I hadn’t survived. That I was a ghost, and my family had no idea I was there. When I asked Barbara to confirm that I existed, she said yes, but that was just the sort of thing a hallucination would say.
几乎临死后又回到世界上并不是人们可能期望的解脱。当时，芭芭拉和我有两个小女孩，一个六个月大，一个三岁，看到她们兴奋地等在保姆身边，当我走上泥泞的车道时，我被抽泣所震撼。我并没有感到欢欣，而是被一种可怕且不理性的恐惧所困扰，担心自己可能没有存活下来。我是一个幽灵，我的家人根本不知道我在那里。当我问芭芭拉确认我是否存在时，她说是的，但这只是一种幻觉才会说的话。

As I sank deeper into existential paranoia, I began to research the psychological effects of almost dying. In literature and history, insanity is not an uncommon result. In Herman Melville’s epic novel “Moby-Dick,” a character named Pip falls off a chase boat in the middle of a dead-calm sea, only to be rescued hours later by sheer luck. “He saw God’s foot upon the treadle of the loom and spoke it; and therefor his shipmates called him mad,” Melville wrote of Pip. “So man’s insanity is heaven’s sense.”
当我陷入更深的存在主义偏执时，我开始研究几乎死亡的心理影响。在文学和历史中，疯狂并不罕见。在赫尔曼·梅尔维尔的史诗小说《白鲸》中，一个名叫皮普的角色在一片死寂的海中从追逐船上掉下来，几个小时后仅仅凭运气被救起。"他看见上帝的脚踩在织布机的踏板上，说出来；因此他的船员们称他为疯子，"梅尔维尔写道。"所以人的疯狂就是天堂的感觉。"

In 1849, an aspiring writer named Fyodor Dostoyevsky was arrested with several other men in St. Petersburg for openly discussing the liberation of serfs. After months of imprisonment, the men were tied to posts before a firing squad in a city square. The soldiers charged their rifles, raised them and waited for the command. “What if I were not to die! What if life were given back to me—what infinity!” Dostoyevsky wrote in his novel “The Idiot,” about a character widely thought to be a stand-in for himself. “I should turn every minute into an age, I should lose nothing, I should count every moment separately and waste none.”
1849 年，一位志向成为作家的费奥多尔·陀思妥耶夫斯基与圣彼得堡的其他几名男子因公开讨论农奴解放而被捕。经过数月的监禁，这些男子被绑在城市广场上的柱子前，面对行刑队。士兵们上膛，举枪等待命令。“如果我不死呢！如果生命重新赐予我——那将是何等的无限！”陀思妥耶夫斯基在他的小说《白痴》中写道，这部小说中的人物被广泛认为是他自己的替身。“我会把每一分钟都变成一个世纪，我不会失去任何东西，我会单独计算每一刻，不浪费任何一刻。”

Dostoyevsky was spared: A horseman cantered into the square in time to announce the czar had pardoned them. Two fellow political prisoners were psychologically destroyed by the experience, but Dostoyevsky found some measure of salvation in it. He discovered a sense of reverence for the actual, moment-to-moment experience of life that remained with him until he died.
陀斯妥耶夫斯基得以幸免：一名骑马的人及时赶到广场上宣布沙皇已经赦免了他们。两名同伴政治犯在这一经历中心理上受到了摧毁，但陀斯妥耶夫斯基在其中找到了某种救赎。他发现了对生活实际、时刻的体验的一种敬畏之感，这种感觉一直伴随着他直到他去世。

Everyone has a relationship with death, whether we want one or not. (Refusing to think about it is its own kind of relationship.) When we learn about another person’s end, the pity we feel is rooted in the hope that that kind of thing—the car accident, the drowning, the cancer—will not, could never happen to us. It is a helpful illusion.
每个人都与死亡有关系，无论我们是否想要。 （拒绝思考它也是一种关系。）当我们了解另一个人的结局时，我们所感到的怜悯是根植于希望那种事情——车祸、溺水、癌症——永远不会发生在我们身上。这是一种有益的幻觉。

Dying is the most ordinary thing you will ever do but also the most radical. You will go from a living, conscious being to dust. Nothing in your life can possibly prepare you for this. It has its own timetable and so requires neither courage nor willingness, though both help enormously.
死亡是你将要经历的最普通的事情，但也是最激进的。你将从一个有生命、有意识的存在变成尘土。你的生活中没有任何事情可以为此做好准备。它有自己的时间表，因此既不需要勇气也不需要意愿，尽管两者都会极大地帮助。

I had cheated death before. I was supposed to cover the unrest in Libya in 2011 with Tim Hetherington, a British photographer and friend from [our years reporting](https://www.wsj.com/articles/BL-SEB-39034?mod=article_inline) on the war in Afghanistan. I had to pull out at the last moment, but Hetherington went on without me and was killed by mortar fire.
我曾经死里逃生。我本应该在 2011 年与英国摄影师、朋友蒂姆·赫瑟灵顿一起报道利比亚的动荡局势，我们曾一起报道阿富汗战争。我不得不在最后一刻退出，但赫瑟灵顿没有我继续前进，却被迫击炮火击毙。

Returning home from his funeral, I found myself inhabiting a different world from the one I’d left—dull, monochromatic and without hope. Against all logic, I had convinced myself that Tim’s death was my fault and that it should have been me. Some days, I caught myself thinking that he was the lucky one to have died before life turned so painful. Things unraveled quickly after that. My first marriage ended. My father died. The best man at my wedding rented a car, drove to a sporting goods store, bought a shotgun and ended his life in a parking lot.
从葬礼回家后，我发现自己生活在一个与我离开时完全不同的世界——沉闷、单调，没有希望。尽管毫无逻辑，我已经说服自己蒂姆的死是我的错，本应该是我。有些日子，我发现自己在想他是幸运的一个，因为他在生活变得如此痛苦之前就去世了。事情在那之后迅速崩溃。我的第一次婚姻结束了。我父亲去世了。我婚礼上的伴郎租了一辆车，开到了一个体育用品店，买了一支猎枪，然后在停车场结束了自己的生命。

One of the core goals of life is survival; the other is meaning. In some ways, they are antithetical. Situations with intense consequences, such as childbirth, combat and natural disasters, are often exceedingly meaningful. Safer situations are usually not. A round of golf may be pleasant (or not) but has very little meaning because almost nothing is at stake. In that context, adrenaline junkies are actually “meaning junkies,” and danger-seekers are actually “consequence-seekers.” Death, the ultimate consequence, is the ultimate reality that gives life meaning.
生活的核心目标之一是生存；另一个是意义。在某种程度上，它们是对立的。像分娩、战斗和自然灾害等后果严重的情况通常具有极大的意义。相对安全的情况通常没有太多意义。打一场高尔夫球可能很愉快（或者不愉快），但几乎没有什么意义，因为几乎没有什么在危险中。在这种情况下，肾上腺素狂人实际上是“意义狂人”，而寻求危险的人实际上是“后果追求者”。死亡，最终的后果，是赋予生命意义的最终现实。

I was supposed to die that otherwise ordinary summer day in 2020, but I didn’t. The extra years that had been returned to me were too terrifying to be beautiful and too precious to be ordinary. A week after I came home, I found myself sitting at a window looking at a crab apple tree in the backyard. The branches were waving in the wind, and I had the thought that they’d be waving in exactly the same way if I’d died—only I wouldn’t be there to see it.
我本应该在 2020 年那个平凡的夏日去世，但我没有。那些被还给我的额外岁月太可怕，无法成为美丽，又太珍贵，无法平凡。回家一周后，我发现自己坐在窗前，看着后院的一棵海棠树。树枝在风中摇曳，我突然想到，如果我死了，它们也会以完全相同的方式摇曳——只是我不会在那里看到。

I continued to worry that I might die. During a follow-up scan, the radiologist spotted a mass against my pancreas, which was probably a clump of old blood and scar tissue, but it could also have been cancer. It is often easy to prove you have cancer, but it turns out it is fairly hard to prove you don’t. As test after test came back inconclusive, I sank into a kind of existential insanity. Every sunset, every dinner, every bedtime story dripped with ghastly significance because I might be dead in three months.
我继续担心我可能会死。在随访扫描期间，放射科医生发现我的胰腺上有一个肿块，可能是一团旧血和瘢痕组织，但也可能是癌症。通常很容易证明你患有癌症，但事实证明证明你没有患癌症却相当困难。随着一次又一次的检查结果无法确定，我陷入了某种存在主义的疯狂状态。每一个日落，每一顿晚餐，每一个入睡故事都充满了可怕的意义，因为也许三个月后我就会死去。

Every day I cycled from an almost drugged state of appreciation to panic and back again. Barbara said she couldn’t take much more of this. She made the excellent point that I had an opportunity to experience the insights of terminal illness without—almost certainly—having to pay the price. What was I learning? My father had continued reading history books until the last weeks of his life. Would I keep practicing music if I found out I was going to die? Reading? Running? What would be the point? But then, what’s the point anyway?
每天我都在近乎麻木的欣赏状态和恐慌之间来回骑车。芭芭拉说她无法再忍受这种情况。她提出了一个很好的观点，即我有机会体验晚期疾病的洞察力，而几乎可以肯定地说，我不必付出代价。我在学到什么？我父亲一直在生命的最后几周里继续阅读历史书。如果我发现自己将要死去，我会继续练习音乐吗？阅读？跑步？有什么意义呢？但是，那又有什么意义呢？

Our universe was created by unknowable forces and has no implicit reason to exist. In such a world, what couldn’t happen? My dead father appearing above me in a trauma bay is the least of it. When I was in the ICU, a nurse suggested I think about my experience as something sacred rather than scary. When I tried to find her later, no one at the hospital knew who she was; no one even knew what I was talking about. It occurred to me that she did not exist.
我们的宇宙是由无法知晓的力量创造的，没有明显的存在理由。在这样一个世界中，有什么是不可能发生的呢？在外伤急救室里，我死去的父亲出现在我上方只是其中最不可能的事情。当我在重症监护室时，一名护士建议我把我的经历看作是神圣的而不是可怕的。当我试图找到她时，医院里没有人知道她是谁；甚至没有人知道我在说什么。我意识到她并不存在。

I’m a lifelong atheist, but I decided that my experience was, in fact, sacred. I couldn’t really know life until I knew death, and I can’t really know death until it comes for me. Without death, life does not require focus or courage or choice. Without death, life is just an extraordinary stunt that won’t stop.
我是一位终身无神论者，但我决定我的经历实际上是神圣的。在我经历死亡之前，我无法真正了解生命，而在死亡降临之前，我也无法真正了解死亡。没有死亡，生命就不需要专注、勇气或选择。没有死亡，生命只是一个不会停止的非凡壮举。
