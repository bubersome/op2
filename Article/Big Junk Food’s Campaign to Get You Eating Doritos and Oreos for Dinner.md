Big Junk Food’s Campaign to Get You Eating Doritos and Oreos for Dinner
大垃圾食品的活动让你晚餐吃多力多滋和奥利奥
Meanwhile, the multibillion-dollar industry is telling the anti-processed foods movement to chill out.

If you’ve been paying even a little attention to health news recently, you’ve likely seen the words “ultra-processed foods” in headlines, blamed for everything from depression to diabetes to early death. Last year the book Ultra-Processed People, a tour through the effects of a factory-bred diet by BBC broadcaster Chris van Tulleken, was widely lauded in reviews by virtually every mainstream news outlet. On a panel in the early fall, renowned food scholar, molecular biologist and author Marion Nestle said the number of studies connecting ultra-processed foods to negative health outcomes had passed the 1,600 mark.
如果你最近稍微关注过健康新闻，你可能会在头条新闻中看到“超加工食品”这个词，人们将其归咎于从抑郁症到糖尿病到过早死亡的各种疾病。去年，英国广播公司 (BBC) 广播员克里斯·范·塔勒肯 (Chris van Tulleken) 所著的《超加工人群》一书讲述了工厂饲养饮食的影响，几乎所有主流新闻媒体都在评论中广受好评。在初秋的一次小组会议上，著名食品学者、分子生物学家兼作家 Marion Nestle 表示，将超加工食品与负面健康结果联系起来的研究数量已超过 1,600 项。

The term, as critics love to point out, is not universally defined, and not all processed foods are equally bad for you. Still, it describes about three-quarters of the American food supply. While it refers broadly to any food made mostly of industrial ingredients that themselves are already highly processed (think pea protein isolate or methylcellulose), some like to define it as foods made with ingredients you wouldn’t find in a home kitchen. I tend to subscribe to a theory articulated by Supreme Court Justice Potter Stewart in 1964 about obscenity: “I know it when I see it.”
正如批评家喜欢指出的那样，这个术语并没有得到普遍定义，而且并非所有加工食品都同样对你有害。尽管如此，它仍然描述了美国约四分之三的食品供应。虽然它泛指主要由本身已经经过高度加工的工业成分制成的任何食品（例如豌豆分离蛋白或甲基纤维素），但有些人喜欢将其定义为由家庭厨房中找不到的成分制成的食品。我倾向于赞同最高法院法官波特·斯图尔特 (Potter Stewart) 在 1964 年阐述的关于淫秽的理论：“当我看到它时，我就知道它。”

The food industry has taken a multipronged approach to combat the backlash. On one hand, companies have been slowly bending to the ongoing consumer demands for healthier foods—simplifying products, shortening ingredients and using phrases like “Better for you” to describe a subset of their offerings. (For the record, a shortened ingredients list doesn’t by any means guarantee that a food is not ultra-processed. But it looks healthier.) With US consumers now buying less in supermarkets—a confluence of trends including inflation, reduced food stamp benefits and maybe even the Ozempic effect—the shift for food companies to once again be in the business of real food seems nothing short of urgent.
食品行业采取了多管齐下的方法来应对这种强烈反对。一方面，公司一直在慢慢地满足消费者对健康食品的持续需求——简化产品、缩短成分，并使用“更好地为你”等短语来描述他们的部分产品。 （根据记录，缩短的成分清单并不能保证食品没有经过超加工。但它看起来更健康。）随着美国消费者现在在超市购买的商品减少，通货膨胀、食品券减少等趋势综合在一起利益，甚至可能是臭氧效应——食品公司再次转向真正的食品业务似乎迫在眉睫。

At the same time they’re lobbying the federal government to view all this anti-processed food research with caution, the Wall Street Journal reported in January, and they’re saying it’s actually the processing that makes their food so vital to American life. They’ve also been outright dismissive of the term. “I try to stay away from buzzwords,” Jeff Harmening, chief executive officer of General Mills Inc., said last summer at an event when asked about the term. Mark Smucker, the fifth-generation CEO of JM Smucker Co., also derided it as a “buzzword” in an interview with Bloomberg News a little more than a week later. A few months after that, his company said it would acquire Hostess Brands, maker of Twinkies. “I don’t like the word ultra-processed, and I don’t believe in it,” PepsiCo Inc. CEO Ramon Laguarta said at the World Economic Forum in January, right after declaring his commitment to “food systems transformation.”
与此同时，据《华尔街日报》一月份报道，他们正在游说联邦政府谨慎对待所有这些反加工食品研究，他们表示，实际上正是加工过程使他们的食品对美国人的生活如此重要。他们也对这个词完全不屑一顾。 “我尽量远离流行语，”通用磨坊公司首席执行官杰夫·哈梅宁去年夏天在一次活动中被问及这个术语时说道。 JM Smucker公司第五代首席执行官Mark Smucker在一周多后接受彭博新闻社采访时也嘲笑它是“流行语”。几个月后，他的公司表示将收购 Twinkies 制造商 Hostess Brands。 “我不喜欢超加工这个词，我也不相信它，”百事可乐公司首席执行官拉蒙·拉瓜塔在一月份的世界经济论坛上宣布致力于“食品系统转型”后表示。

Transformation was not exactly the vibe at a Boca Raton, Florida, luxury beachside resort in February, where the country’s biggest makers of packaged foods told Wall Street investors about their growth plans for the coming year. Company after company at the annual Consumer Analyst Group of New York conference said it wants to make more junk food more available to more people more often. The business-casual crowd started the day at sponsored breakfasts that offered dozens of kinds of cereal and between presentations sat in a coffee lounge where the tables were covered with packaged cupcakes. At one particularly memorable Mondelez International Inc.-sponsored snack break, they followed tradition and swarmed a massive pile of Cadbury Eggs and Lu Le Pims biscuits.
二月份，佛罗里达州博卡拉顿豪华海滨度假胜地的氛围并不完全是转型，该国最大的包装食品制造商向华尔街投资者介绍了他们来年的增长计划。一家又一家的公司在纽约消费者分析师组织年度会议上表示，希望更频繁地向更多人提供更多垃圾食品。商务休闲人群以提供数十种麦片的赞助早餐开始新的一天，在演讲间隙坐在咖啡厅里，桌子上摆满了包装好的纸杯蛋糕。在亿滋国际公司 (Mondelez International Inc.) 赞助的一次特别难忘的小吃休息时间，他们遵循传统，蜂拥而至一大堆吉百利鸡蛋 (Cadbury Eggs) 和 Lu Le Pims 饼干。

From the stage, executives unveiled ultra-processed creations, practically suggesting they become part of the new food pyramid. “We think our products belong (and we’re seeing that already) in home—both as side dishes but also as ingredients in the meal,” Laguarta said onstage, next to a slide showing Doritos and Tostitos.
高管们在舞台上展示了超加工食品，实际上表明它们成为新食品金字塔的一部分。 “我们认为我们的产品适合（而且我们已经看到）适合家庭使用——既可以作为配菜，也可以作为膳食中的配料，”拉瓜塔在舞台上说道，旁边是一张展示多力多滋和托斯蒂托的幻灯片。

Expanding into new “occasions” was the theme of the conference. PepsiCo, the largest retail food company in the country with more than $29 billion in US food sales alone, wants to do so by putting Doritos on the dinner table. Cereal, which has been falling out of favor for more than a decade as Americans opt for yogurts, bars, fast-food breakfast sandwiches or sometimes nothing at all, shouldn’t be confined to the morning, said Gary Pilnick, CEO of cereal company WK Kellogg Co. Froot Loops and Frosted Flakes are perfectly sensible as dinner or on-the-go snacks, now packaged in convenient single-serving bags, he said.
扩展到新的“场合”是会议的主题。百事公司是美国最大的零售食品公司，仅在美国的食品销售额就超过 290 亿美元，它希望通过将多力多滋放在餐桌上来实现这一目标。谷类食品公司首席执行官加里·皮尔尼克 (Gary Pilnick) 表示，十多年来，随着美国人选择酸奶、营养棒、快餐早餐三明治，有时甚至什么也不吃，谷类食品已经不再受青睐，但谷物食品不应该只局限于早上。他说，WK Kellogg Co. 的 Froot Loops 和 Frosted Flakes 非常适合作为晚餐或随身携带的零食，现在包装在方便的单份袋中。

Snacking, after all, has been one of the few bright spots for the struggling industry, leading food companies to race to fill every moment of the day with their “innovations”—more junk. And it appears to be working. Mondelez’s 2023 State of Snacking report noted an increase in snacks replacing actual meals. All of this grazing on salt, sugar and fat added about $15 billion to the company’s market capitalization from the end of 2020 to the end of 2023.
毕竟，零食一直是苦苦挣扎的行业中为数不多的亮点之一，导致食品公司竞相用他们的“创新”——更多的垃圾——来填补一天中的每一刻。它似乎正在发挥作用。亿滋国际 (Mondelez) 的 2023 年零食状况报告指出，零食替代正餐的情况有所增加。从 2020 年底到 2023 年底，所有这些对盐、糖和脂肪的消耗使该公司的市值增加了约 150 亿美元。

Which is why the Consumer Brands Association is urging people to reconsider processed foods as less of an enemy and more of a gift. “Processing gives us that time back that we can use to create new memories,” wrote David Chavern, CEO of the CBA, the Big Food interest group that represents all of these companies, in an op-ed last winter. Apparently, he meant memories created over a bag of chips and some Oreos shared at the dinner table.
这就是为什么消费者品牌协会敦促人们重新考虑加工食品，不再将其视为敌人，而将其视为礼物。 “加工给了我们时间，我们可以用它来创造新的记忆，”代表所有这些公司的大型食品利益集团 CBA 的首席执行官 David Chavern 在去年冬天的一篇专栏文章中写道。显然，他指的是在餐桌上分享的一袋薯片和一些奥利奥所创造的回忆。

A Hedge Fund Billionaire’s Cash Helped Fund a ‘Predatory’ Lender
One man started a cash-advance company. The other was a retired investing legend. A private credit fund had what both were looking for.








Leon Cooperman at home in Boca Raton, Florida, in 2022.
Leon Cooperman at home in Boca Raton, Florida, in 2022.Photographer: Scott McIntyre/for Getty Images/The Washington Post
By Zachary R Mider and Zeke Faux
April 23, 2024 at 4:00 AM CDT

Save
The two men come from the opposite ends of Wall Street.

David Glass inspired the stock-scam movie Boiler Room, avoided prison in an insider-trading case by wearing a wire, then started a boiler room of his own. His company stands accused of ripping off small businesses on an industrial scale, collecting more than $1 billion of illegal interest over a decade.


Vin Diesel in Boiler Room.
Vin Diesel in Boiler Room.Photographer: Alamy
Leon Cooperman rose to a senior post at Goldman Sachs, then founded a hedge fund that made him a billionaire. He became a noted philanthropist, with his name on hospital buildings in New Jersey and Florida. He calls himself a “capitalist with a heart.”

There’s no indication the two men know each other. But they are linked through the private credit market, one of the hottest corners of Wall Street. A hunt for higher yields has seen wealthy individuals and institutions pour money into loans made outside the banking system. Whether these investors are aware or not, some of that money has found its way to businesses that prey on the most desperate.

In Cooperman’s case, his foundation has invested more than $100 million with Basepoint Capital LLC, a private credit firm run by a protégé. Basepoint sent some of that money, in turn, to a New Jersey lender called Delta Bridge Funding LLC. According to the New York attorney general’s office, Delta Bridge is the latest incarnation of Glass’ boiler room outfit, which it calls “a large-scale predatory lending operation.”

The connection was revealed last month when New York Attorney General Letitia James filed a lawsuit against Delta Bridge and others seeking $1.4 billion over allegations of illegal lending at interest rates as high as 820% a year. Basepoint was not accused of wrongdoing, and Cooperman wasn’t mentioned.

Reached by phone at his home in Florida, Cooperman says he doesn’t know anything about Delta Bridge and hadn’t heard about the lawsuit until Bloomberg Businessweek called. He’s just one of many Basepoint investors, he says, and plays no role in deciding which firms it backs. “Good luck,” he says. “I don’t know anything about it.”

Basepoint said in a statement that it is “confident in our efforts to ensure the quality of the compliance programs implemented by our counterparties.” A lawyer for Delta Bridge said it has a “documented commitment to being an industry leader in compliance.” Glass didn’t respond to requests for comment.


Leon Cooperman in his home office.
Cooperman, who founded hedge fund Omega Advisors, still manages his own money and that of his family foundation.Photographer: Scott McIntyre/for Getty Images/The Washington Post
Cooperman, 80, has pledged to give away more than 90% of his wealth, which is estimated at $2.7 billion, according to the Bloomberg Billionaires Index. In a 2022 profile in the Washington Post, he expressed confusion about why billionaires like him receive public criticism, since his wealth is the result of hard work. He appeared on CNBC last year to promote his memoir, which he said he wrote to make sure young people like his grandchildren learn the merits of capitalism. At one point, Cooperman choked back tears when talking about how he was able to rise from humble beginnings.

“I made a lot of money, and I’m giving it all back,” he said, pausing to compose himself.

Although he quit managing money for others years ago, Cooperman still tends to his fortune and that of the Leon and Toby Cooperman Family Foundation. More than a quarter of the foundation’s assets—some $143 million—were invested in Basepoint or its funds as of January 2022, according to the most recent full tax filings. Some $9 million of that was in funds backing Delta Bridge.

That confluence of high and low finance came in 2021. Glass’ original firm, Yellowstone Capital LLC, was in trouble. The Federal Trade Commission and attorneys general in two states were investigating its lending practices. Partly because of that scrutiny, a South Korean company was threatening to yank its credit line, which would have meant lights out.

Yellowstone grew out of the financial crisis a decade earlier. Small businesses such as pizza parlors and barbershops were having trouble getting loans from banks. A new industry called merchant cash advance arose, with salesmen pitching short-term money at annualized interest rates of 300% or more. The paperwork governing these deals was designed to sidestep state usury laws by characterizing the transactions as advances on future revenue rather than loans.

Operating in a legal vacuum, the industry attracted characters whose résumés wouldn’t get a second glance from a recruiter at Goldman Sachs: convicted stock fraudsters like Glass, mortgage salesmen banned from the industry for scams, even mob associates. Yellowstone and its ilk flooded the New York court system with so many dubious collection lawsuits that the state had to change its laws to stop them.

The sky-high interest rates and aggressive collection tactics trapped many Yellowstone borrowers in cycles of debt. “The only way we could afford to make the payments was to take on even more debt—often from Yellowstone itself,” the founder of City Bakery, a Manhattan institution known for its pretzel croissants, said in a court filing. That business took seven cash advances from Yellowstone before it folded. After a Yellowstone executive told a plumber in Virginia that death or winning the lottery was the only escape from his crushing debts, the borrower walked into the woods and tried to kill himself.

At Yellowstone’s headquarters in Jersey City, New Jersey, business was good. At its peak, the company was lending more than $500 million a year. In a text message obtained by the attorney general, one executive estimated that the place minted at least 30 millionaires.

But Yellowstone’s size made it an obvious target for law enforcement. Investigators were looking at whether the company was systematically charging merchants more than they owed, and whether the deals themselves were actually loans in disguise, which would make the eye-popping interest rates illegal.

So, Glass worked out a plan, according to the New York attorney general’s suit: A group of Yellowstone executives—but not Glass or Isaac Stern, the chief executive officer—would form a new company, take on Yellowstone’s assets and employees, and continue operations under a new name, Delta Bridge. Yellowstone itself would go out of business.


New York Attorney General Letitia James filed a $1.4 billion lawsuit against merchant cash-advance companies in March.
New York Attorney General Letitia James filed a $1.4 billion lawsuit against merchant cash-advance companies in March.Photographer: Michael M. Santiago/Getty Images
The attorney general says the deal was designed to stiff government investigators and creditors by leaving behind only a defunct company with minimal assets. (In a deposition, Glass said the opposite: that it was designed to maximize what was left to pay debts.)

To complete the deal, Delta Bridge would need a credit line to fund its operations. That’s where Basepoint came in.

Like Yellowstone, Basepoint sprang up after the financial crisis to provide credit in places where banks were retreating. Rather than make loans directly, though, Basepoint offers financing to other lenders from its offices overlooking Manhattan’s Rockefeller Center.

It’s easy to see why Basepoint can charge clients more interest than a typical bank would, and why those clients might have trouble getting funding elsewhere. Some are cash-advance companies. Others are payday lenders that skirt state usury laws using tribal connections. Some offer loans against car titles or run pawnshops.

One Basepoint client is run by a convicted smuggler, another by a man the US Securities and Exchange Commission wants banned from trading penny stocks. A former client was ordered to pay $157 million after a judge found it had deceived customers and violated usury laws in a dozen states.

Cooperman’s relationship with Basepoint dates to 2015, when his hedge fund, Omega Advisors Inc., invested in one of the company’s deals. In 2020, one of Cooperman’s former lieutenants at Omega, Eric Schneider, took over Basepoint. He later combined it with Puerto Rico-based Advantage Insurance Inc., forming a financial group with $6 billion in assets. Cooperman helped finance the deal and became a part owner of the combined company, according to a person with direct knowledge of the transaction who requested anonymity because Advantage’s owners aren’t publicly disclosed. Schneider declined to comment.

Meanwhile, thanks to Basepoint’s backing, Glass’ plan came to fruition in May 2021. On a Friday, Yellowstone officially closed its doors; on Monday, most of the same employees returned to the same office, rechristened as Delta Bridge.

The attorney general says that illegal lending continued after the transition and that Stern and Glass remained involved. “Delta Bridge is the same business as Yellowstone,” the lawsuit says, just with “a different name.”

The Delta Bridge lawyer, Lee Vartan, said in an email that’s not true. “Delta Bridge is an independent business where Isaac Stern and David Glass were never involved,” he said. “It’s unfortunate the attorney general decided to generate misleading headlines rather than tell the truth about our documented commitment to being an industry leader in compliance, which we will look forward to proving in court.”
三角洲大桥律师李·瓦尔坦 (Lee Vartan) 在一封电子邮件中表示，这不是事实。 “三角洲大桥是一家独立企业，艾萨克·斯特恩和大卫·格拉斯从未参与其中，”他说。 “不幸的是，总检察长决定制造误导性的头条新闻，而不是说出我们书面承诺成为合规行业领导者的真相，我们将期待在法庭上证明这一点。”

Months later, Basepoint helped the new Delta Bridge leadership come up with $25 million to pay off a debt to Yellowstone. Basepoint reported in securities filings that the funds financing Delta Bridge had raised $229 million from investors. That includes the $9 million investment that Cooperman’s foundation said it held as of 2022. Any Cooperman investments outside of the foundation wouldn’t have to be disclosed.
几个月后，Basepoint 帮助三角洲大桥新领导层筹集了 2500 万美元来偿还黄石公园的债务。 Basepoint 在证券备案文件中报告称，为 Delta Bridge 融资的基金已从投资者那里筹集了 2.29 亿美元。其中包括库珀曼基金会声称截至 2022 年持有的 900 万美元投资。库珀曼基金会以外的任何投资都不必披露。

Filed in state court in Manhattan, the lawsuit, in addition to Delta Bridge, targets Yellowstone and 10 executives including Glass and Stern. Five days after it was filed, Delta Bridge informed its business partners that it was halting new loans. The next day it fired dozens of employees.
该诉讼向曼哈顿州法院提起，除三角洲大桥外，还针对黄石公园以及包括格拉斯和斯特恩在内的 10 名高管。提交申请五天后，三角洲大桥通知其业务合作伙伴，它将停止新贷款。第二天，该公司解雇了数十名员工。

Basepoint says it was kept in the dark about what was going on at Delta Bridge. “We would not have provided financing had we known that Delta Bridge secretly (or not secretly) intended to involve Glass or Stern,” Basepoint said in its statement, “or that Delta did not intend to follow the terms of legally compliant MCA agreements and written reconciliation policies.”
Basepoint 表示，他们对 Delta Bridge 发生的事情一无所知。 Basepoint 在声明中表示：“如果我们知道 Delta Bridge 秘密（或非秘密）打算让 Glass 或 Stern 参与其中，或者 Delta 不打算遵守合法的 MCA 协议条款，我们就不会提供融资。”书面和解政策。”

Two people in the cash-advance industry who have received funding from Basepoint and asked that their names not be used because the deals are confidential, said they paid Basepoint an annualized yield of 15% or more. Basepoint paid its investors about 10% a year on some deals, according to securities filings.
两名接受过 Basepoint 融资的现金预支行业人士表示，他们向 Basepoint 支付了 15% 或更高的年化收益率，他们因交易保密而要求不透露姓名。根据证券备案文件，Basepoint 在某些交易中每年向投资者支付约 10% 的费用。

Cooperman has said that he keeps investing only so he can make more money to give away. In speeches and interviews, he likes to tell stories about his upbringing in the Bronx, where he was born to Polish immigrants. He went to public school there and as a teenager worked at jobs that paid him as little as 55¢ an hour.
库珀曼表示，他继续投资只是为了赚更多的钱来捐赠。在演讲和采访中，他喜欢讲述自己在布朗克斯长大的故事，他的父母是波兰移民。他在那里上公立学校，青少年时期从事的工作每小时工资只有 55 美分。

“That’s the American dream,” he once said. “Come from nothing, make a lot of money and share that success with others less fortunate.”
“这就是美国梦，”他曾经说过。 “白手起家，赚很多钱，并与其他不幸的人分享成功。”

China’s Bubble Tea Boom Creates a Half-Dozen Billionaires
Sellers of the hugely popular drink are rushing to cash in, but increasing competition threatens to spoil the sugar rush.

By Pui Gwen Yeung and Venus Feng
April 21, 2024 at 4:00 PM CDT

Are you a fan of bubble tea? Love it or hate it, you probably know a millennial or Gen Zer who’s crazy about the cloyingly sweet drink filled with sticky balls of tapioca (the “bubbles,” or “boba”) that make it almost chewy as it slithers down your throat. That growing passion has created at least a half-dozen billionaires in China in the past few years and is at the heart of several other potential fortunes.

On April 23, Sichuan Baicha Baidao Industrial Co., China’s No. 3 bubble tea chain, is scheduled to start trading, aiming to raise more than $300 million in the biggest Hong Kong listing since November. That valuation would give its husband-and-wife founders, Wang Xiaokun and Liu Weihong, a combined net worth of $2.7 billion based on the 73% stake they’ll own after the initial public offering, according to the Bloomberg Billionaires Index.


Baicha Baidao’s shops are also known as Chabaidao (“100 Varieties of Tea”) in China.
Baicha Baidao’s shops are also known as Chabaidao (“100 Varieties of Tea”) in China.Source: Baicha Baidao
Despite Baicha Baidao’s confidence, the market debuts of several other Chinese bubble tea makers that have hinted at Hong Kong IPOs are in limbo. Growing competition threatens to take out some of the weaker players, and Hong Kong—once among the busiest venues for deals—has lost its appeal as shares have slumped in China’s economic downturn. “The market is not giving this sector as lofty valuations as before,” says Kenny Ng, a strategist at Everbright Securities International. “There’s been an uneven revival of consumption in mainland China, so the profitability of consumer businesses remains uncertain.”

Bubble tea was invented in Taiwan in the late 1980s, with small stalls selling it near schools and offices. As the trend started to take root in Hong Kong and mainland China in the ’90s, chains began springing up. Today, thousands of brands vie for the attention of thirsty tea lovers across China, and countless shops have popped up in the US and Europe, though the biggest Chinese providers don’t sell there—yet.

Like Starbucks Corp., these chains offer an almost infinite number of combinations. You can pair your favorite tea (any black, white, green or pu’er variety, with or without milk) with fresh fruit (mulberry, grapefruit, strawberry, orange), various bubbles (the original dark tapioca balls, crystal, cactus, taro and more), special additions (red beans, chunks of flavored gelatin, a topping of cream cheese foam) and various levels of sweetness and iciness. For an extra kick, some chains offer a shot of Moutai, the traditional Chinese grain liquor. “Life is tough, and something sweet will make you happier,” was the top response to the question “Why do you buy tea drinks?” in a survey cited by Minsheng Securities.

Baicha Baidao, also known as Chabaidao (“100 Varieties of Tea”), was built on serving price-conscious fans of the stuff. The founders opened their first outlet in 2008, a 20-square-meter (215-square-foot) shop near a middle school in Chengdu. In 2018 they introduced a franchise model that turbocharged growth, and today the company has more than 8,000 shops across China. In January, Baicha Baidao opened an outlet in Seoul, its first outside China, and at home it introduced its first coffee shop, Coffree.

Baicha Baidao’s stores have long sold a half-liter serving of bubble tea for a bit more than $2, while the industry average until recently was closer to $5. The strategy has paid off, with sales jumping more than 56% between 2021 and 2023, to 5.7 billion yuan ($787 million), according to Baicha Baidao’s IPO prospectus. The company declined to comment for this story.

Two other bubble tea billionaires are Zhang Hongchao and Zhang Hongfu, brothers who in 1997 founded Mixue Bingcheng in Henan province. Mixue Group (which also has a coffee brand) calls itself the world’s second-largest drink chain after Starbucks by number of stores, with more than 32,000 outlets across China and 4,000 in 11 other countries, mostly nearby. After a 2020 cash infusion from investors, including the venture arm of Chinese food delivery giant Meituan and Hillhouse Investment Management, the tea maker was valued at 23.3 billion yuan, pushing the net worth of each brother to $1.5 billion, according to the Bloomberg Billionaires Index.
另外两位珍珠奶茶亿万富翁是张洪超和张洪福兄弟，他们于1997年在河南省创立了蜜雪冰城。米雪集团（也有一个咖啡品牌）自称是全球第二大饮料连锁店，按门店数量计算，仅次于星巴克，在中国拥有超过 32,000 家门店，在其他 11 个国家（大部分位于附近）拥有 4,000 家门店。根据彭博亿万富翁指数，在中国食品配送巨头美团点评和高瓴投资管理公司等投资者 2020 年注入现金后，这家茶叶制造商的估值达到 233 亿元人民币，将兄弟俩的净资产推至 15 亿美元。

With China’s economy slowing and inflation-weary consumers tightening their belts, more producers are cutting prices. Guming Holdings Ltd. (China’s second-largest player, with some 9,000 stores) and No. 4 Auntea Jenny (Shanghai) Industrial Co., which have also filed IPO documents in Hong Kong, now charge less than $3.50 per cup. “Competition has gotten fierce,” Gloria Cao, an analyst at Essence International, wrote in an April 16 report.
随着中国经济放缓以及厌倦通胀的消费者勒紧裤腰带，更多生产商开始降价。古明控股有限公司（中国第二大企业，拥有约 9,000 家门店）和第四大阿姨珍妮（上海）实业有限公司也在香港提交了 IPO 文件，目前每杯售价低于 3.5 美元。 “竞争已经变得激烈，”安信国际分析师 Gloria Cao 在 4 月 16 日的一份报告中写道。

Higher-end tea makers such as Nayuki Holdings Ltd.—an 1,800-store chain that went public in Hong Kong three years ago—have suffered from the rapid growth of cheaper rivals. Nayuki, which reduced the price of a cup to about $2.50, has seen its shares tumble almost 90% since their listing on concerns over competition, and its once-billionaire founders, Peng Xin and husband Zhao Lin, are now worth less than $300 million, down from $2.2 billion in 2021. “I’m optimistic about the prospects of the industry leaders,” says Steven Nie, an analyst at Daiwa Capital Markets. “But the fresh tea drinks sector is getting too crowded.”
奈雪控股有限公司（Nayuki Holdings Ltd.，一家拥有 1,800 家门店的连锁店，三年前在香港上市）等高端茶制造商因廉价竞争对手的快速增长而遭受损失。 Nayuki 将杯子的价格降至 2.5 美元左右，但由于对竞争的担忧，其股价自上市以来已下跌近 90%，其曾经的亿万富翁创始人彭欣和丈夫赵琳现在的身家不足 3 亿美元，低于 2021 年的 22 亿美元。“我对行业领导者的前景感到乐观，”大和资本市场分析师 Steven Nie 表示。 “但新鲜茶饮料行业变得过于拥挤。”
