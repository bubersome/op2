# 新型循环：氧燃烧涡轮机循环系统

G。理查兹，美国国家能源技术实验室

## 摘要：

本章讨论了氧气燃料燃烧系统的最新发展，以建立近零排放发电厂来控制二氧化碳排放。本综述的重点是基于氧燃料涡轮机的联合循环系统，而不是粉煤锅炉的氧气燃料方法。这些动力循环试图利用二氧化碳与氧燃料燃烧产品流的直接分离，作为在发电过程中管理二氧化碳排放的方法。本文讨论了一些新的氧燃料循环配置，并描述了实现高效率所需的组件运行条件和性能。总结了氧燃料燃烧器、涡轮机和热交换器的现状，讨论了开发用于氧燃料应用的涡轮机时需要考虑的要点。虽然文献中提出了各种不同的循环配置，但其中一些循环差异集中在主导工作流体（CO2或水）的选择以及循环如何利用从加热流体中提取工作。与现有的燃气轮机相比，工作流体的变化可能需要专门制造的组件来达到预期的效率。此外，这些循环中的燃烧系统与传统天然气组合循环中的燃烧系统有很大不同，因为氧燃料工艺设计理想情况下可以在没有多余的氧气的情况下运行。讨论了这些和其他差异的影响。

## 6.1 导言

在过去十年中，氧燃料燃烧作为发电过程中二氧化碳管理方法的显著关注。这个概念很简单：在燃烧过程中使用氧气而不是空气。使用氧燃料方法，任何碳氢化合物的理想燃烧产物只是二氧化碳和氢气，导致非常简单的二氧化碳去除，因为水很容易冷凝。对于煤粉锅炉，正在重大研究和演示工作（[Figueroa*等人*，2008年](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0115)）和演示工作（[Cook，2009年](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0095)；[Stromberg*等人*，2009年](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0295)）中探索氧气方法，因为它为从传统锅炉设计中清除二氧化碳的烟气提供了替代方案。在实践中，氧气燃料锅炉的开发因需要提供纯氧而不是空气而变得复杂，因此必须仔细考虑将大型空气分离装置（ASU）与该过程的其余部分集成。供应氧气所需的能源很重要，但亚利桑那州立大学的先进设计预计将减少氧气生产所需的能源（[Klosterman和Kather，2010年](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0195)），亚利桑那州立大学和动力循环的仔细集成预计将为循环效率带来进一步的好处（[Trainier*等人*，2010年](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0300)）。

鉴于固体燃料发电厂的安装基础庞大，对氧燃料锅炉的兴趣是可以理解的。虽然蒸汽发电厂在提高效率方面得到了高度发展，但它们受到兰金循环配置施加的性能限制的限制。正如本书其他地方所讨论的，组合循环的一个关键优势是，两种技术的结合可以比Rankine循环配置以更高的整体效率运行。作为抵消二氧化碳分离所需的与氧气生产相关的能量惩罚的一种手段，潜在的效率效益是非常可取的。这一潜在的效率优势解释了本章讨论的氧燃料联合循环的拟议发展。如图所示，已经提出了多种流程配置，以利用提高效率的潜力。

本章按以下方式排列。[第6.2节](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#s0015)介绍了最常见的氧燃料动力循环，并比较了最近一些描述先进氧燃料动力循环的论文。[第6.3节](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#s0020)描述了专门研究构建氧燃料循环所需的一些关键组件，特别是在氧燃料环境中运行所需的燃烧室和涡轮膨胀器。[第6.4节](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#s0040)对拟议周期的运作提出了一些评论，第[6.5节](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#s0045)是摘要。



## 6.2 氧气燃料功率循环配置

[图6.1](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#f0010)显示了两种主要类型的氧燃料动力循环的简单示意图。左边（[图6.1a](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#f0010)），干净的碳氢化合物CxHy在高压燃烧室中用氧气和再生水燃烧。注入的水用于控制峰值温度，产生蒸汽和二氧化碳，可以在涡轮机中膨胀，如图所示。离开涡轮机后，气体可以进一步冷却，以便将冷凝水返回燃烧室进行温度控制，而分离的二氧化碳可以压缩并送往地质储存。[图](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#f0010)中的循环[。6.1a](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#f0010)本质上是一个兰金循环，但蒸汽不是锅炉，而是通过直接燃烧产生的。与传统的兰金循环相比，蒸汽工作流体中适量的二氧化碳（体积<10%）使冷凝复杂化，因为非冷凝气体提高了冷凝器中的体积流量。

![肖像](https://learning.oreilly.com/api/v2/epubs/urn:orm:book:9780857090133/files/images/B9780857090133500067_f06-01-9780857090133.jpg)

**6.1** 含氢气燃料回收（a）和二氧化碳回收（b）的氧燃料动力循环。

[图6.1b](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#f0010)显示了第二个氧燃料动力循环。碳氢化合物再次在压力下用氧气燃烧，但二氧化碳而不是水被回收来控制火焰温度。在这种配置中，工作流体主要是二氧化碳，蒸汽水平适中（体积<10%）。燃烧产品通过涡轮机膨胀以产生电力。涡轮排气进一步冷却，允许水去除和分离产生的二氧化碳。剩余的二氧化碳被压缩并返回燃烧室。循环配置本质上是一个半封闭的布雷顿循环，压缩和膨胀以直接类似于燃气轮机的方式发生，但使用不同的工作流体。

[图](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#f0010)中的冷却步骤[。6.1b](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#f0010)可以通过热回收蒸汽发生器（HRSG）来完成，这反过来又将驱动单独的兰金循环。这个概念如[图](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#f0015)所示[。6.2](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#f0015)。该过程看起来与本书其他地方讨论的天然气联合循环（NGCC）非常相似，只是布雷顿循环的工作流体是二氧化碳和水蒸气的混合物，氧化剂是纯氧。这些差异引入了性能处罚和设计挑战，本章将对此进行讨论。为了与其他文献保持一致，[图](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#f0015)中的过程[。6.2](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#f0015)将被称为半封闭式氧燃烧联合循环（SCOC-CC）。

![肖像](https://learning.oreilly.com/api/v2/epubs/urn:orm:book:9780857090133/files/images/B9780857090133500067_f06-02-9780857090133.jpg)

**6.2** 氧气燃料动力循环，二氧化碳回收和HRSG为兰金循环提供热量。这个周期通常被称为SCOC-CC。

也可以开发[图](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#f0010)中所示的更高效的循环版本[。6.1a](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#f0010)其中水蒸气是主要工作流体。对循环最直接的修改是包括一个或多个再加热级，类似于传统的发电厂蒸汽循环。[图](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#f0020)中显示了包括一个额外再加热阶段的工艺图[。6.3](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#f0020)。由于工作流体是燃烧产物，因此只需在随后的再加热阶段燃烧更多的燃料和氧气并继续膨胀过程，就可以重新加热液体。原则上，这种方法可以导致效率比传统的蒸汽动力循环高得多，因为涡轮进气温度不受锅炉管温度限制的限制。相反，工作流体中的燃烧允许将温度设置为涡轮机实际允许的高度，类似于燃气轮机技术。这个循环的实际发展需要能够与高温蒸汽一起运行的涡轮机，而不是燃气轮机中的空气。[图](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#f0020)中的循环[。6.3](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#f0020)不是一个真正的合并周期，但它包含在本讨论中，因为它与本章中描述的零排放概念密切相关。该概念由商业企业家广泛发展，并记录在一系列论文中（[Hustad*等人*，2005年](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0150)；[Pronske*等人*，2006年](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0255)；[Anderson*等人*，2008年，2009年，](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0020)[2010/\](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0030)）。

**6.3** 具有H2O回收和再加热阶段的氧燃料动力循环。（*来源*：改编自Pronske*等人*，200。）

另一个先进的氧燃料循环如[图](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#f0025)所示[。6.4](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#f0025)。在对提出该概念的人的名字进行收缩时，该概念也被称为MATIANT周期（[Yantovski和Mathieu，1996年](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0320)；[Mathieu和Nihart，1999年b](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0225)）。该循环使用超临界二氧化碳作为工作流体，并与兰金循环共享特征，因为二氧化碳被凝结为液态进行低温热排斥。与传统的蒸汽循环不同，随后的相变回气态是通过再生热交换器完成的——这与标准蒸汽兰金循环中的给水预热进行了适度的类比。与其他概念一样，在循环的高压点，将热量添加到循环中的高压点中，该高压点为200巴——类似于蒸汽动力循环压力。

**6.4**（原始）MATIANT氧燃料动力循环，在再加压前使用高压CO2工作流体冷凝到液态。*资料来源*：改编自[Mathieu和Nihart，1999b](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0225)。

[图6.5](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#f0030)显示了另一种氧气燃料循环配置，通常称为格拉茨循环。这种动力循环使用二氧化碳和水蒸气的混合物作为工作流体，并进行了广泛分析，并在一系列论文中提出了几项改进建议（[Jericha和Fesharaki，1995年](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0155)；[Jericha和Göttlich，2002年](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0160)；[Jericha*等人*，2004年，2006年，2008年](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0165)；[Sanz*等人*，2005a](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0170)，[2005b](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0175)，2007年，[2010年](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0275)）。[图](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#f0030)中显示的周期的新颖性[。6.5](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#f0030)是，来自顶部布雷顿循环高温涡轮机的排气热用于在HRSG中产生蒸汽。这种产生的蒸汽在高压涡轮机（HPT）中膨胀，随后用于稀释布雷顿循环中的氧燃料燃烧。离开HRSG的大部分气体被重新压缩并返回燃烧室——这是主要的工作流体。虽然蒸汽再压缩是一个新颖的建议，但它具有通过冷凝热减少循环耐热的潜在效率优势。在[图中。6.5](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#f0030)，在水凝结之前，未返回压缩机的工作流体通过低压涡轮机进一步膨胀，二氧化碳产品被压缩储存。由于存在不可冷凝气体（CO2），这种低压涡轮机和冷凝器的配置与传统的蒸汽循环不同。不可冷凝气体会影响传热和真空泵的配置。为了尽量减少这些并发症，提出了一些版本的格拉茨循环，使用闭环低压底层循环（[Jericha*等人*，2006年](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0170)），其中低压蒸汽的热量由HRSG提供。与其他氧气燃料概念一样，各种文献分析的目标是提高效率，同时保留简单的设计和二氧化碳分离，以便提出不同版本的格拉茨循环，具有不同水平的二氧化碳和H2O回收安排，从33%的蒸汽到75%的蒸汽，以优化循环效率。蒸汽/二氧化碳涡轮机所需组件的详细信息见第[6.3节](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#s0020)。

标准MATIANT气体循环（[Mathieu和Nihart，1999a](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0220)）具有超临界部分与再生CO2 Brayton循环和再加热相结合。燃烧沿着同位线发生。燃料在燃烧器中与工作流体二氧化碳和O2的混合物一起在燃烧器中注入。在燃烧室出口（1300°C），混合物CO2/H2O在第二个燃烧室中重新加热到温度，将其他部分燃料和O2注入其中，然后膨胀。在MATIANT概念中，去除的二氧化碳是液体或超临界的，并且处于高压下。它可用于需要加压二氧化碳的应用。

[Fiaschi*等人（*2009年）](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0110)提出的带叶片冷却的MATIANT循环具有带再加热的超临界Brayton循环和带再加热（叶片冷却）的蒸汽底层循环作为底层循环。燃气轮机叶片冷却不能使用压缩机二氧化碳流量的一小部分进行，因为在如此高的压力和温度下，在叶片通道中注入二氧化碳等流体在技术上会很复杂。CO2 GT的效率比类似的“气燃气”燃气轮机（air-GT）低5%以上，这就是为什么作者在系统设计中实现了闭环蒸汽叶片冷却。

E-MATIANT循环（[Mathieu，2004年](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0215)）使用氧气燃料混合物，包括燃料、亚利桑那州立大学生产的O2和CO2作为工作流体和热调节剂。二氧化碳是回收的。燃烧产物主要是H2O和CO2以及一些O2。H2O通过冷凝去除，燃烧中产生的多余二氧化碳以超临界状态去除。燃烧在1300°C下进行。在这个循环中，涡轮膨胀器排气温度（900-1000°C）高于在空中运行的标准涡轮机（550°C）。

混合SOFC/E-MATIANT循环（[Mathieu，2004年](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0215)）将高温SOFC与上述E-MATIANT循环相结合。系统中有出色的温度匹配。这是一种更高效的强大混合动力车，NG效率约为47-48%。

一些人认为，MATIANT循环（[Mathieu和Nihart，1999b](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0225)）只需要很少新设计的组件，只需要CO2/H2O分离器。它有各种各样的可能应用，包括电力和热电联产、加压流化床燃烧（PFBC）、取代LP膨胀器的磁流体动力学（MHD）、干冰的联合生产、氢气生产和使用金属氧化物而不是ASU的化学循环燃烧。

液化天然气（LNG）准组合超临界二氧化碳兰金循环和二氧化碳布雷顿循环与恢复相互连接（[张和利奥，2006年](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0325)）可以提高液化天然气的65-68%的效率。该系统发电，蒸发液化天然气供进一步使用，同时防止超过50%的液化天然气在蒸发过程中被浪费，并生产液化二氧化碳和水作为燃烧产物，液氮和氩作为ASU产品。

改装成O2/CO2循环的NGCC预计较低加热值（LHV）为51.3%（[Amann*等人*，2009年](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0010)）。这些结果与适用于NGCC的常规胺洗涤进行了比较，并用胺洗涤进行燃烧后捕获。精益二氧化碳负载为0.16摩尔二氧化碳/摩尔胺，该工艺净电效率为49.1%（LHV）。转换成O2/CO2循环似乎更有效率。

提出了一种由化学回收循环和二氧化碳-NG（天然气）重整和氨吸收制冷循环组成的新型动力循环系统（[曹和郑，2006年，](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0045)[2007年](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0050)）。

已经开发了两例先进的氧燃料动力循环，效率为50%（[Gou*等人*，2006年](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0125)）。案例1基本上包括水蒸汽兰金循环和蒸汽-二氧化碳恢复-再加热循环。案例2集成了案例1和顶级布雷顿周期的一些特征。MATIANT和CES周期在常见假设下作为参考进行了评估。与MATIANT和CES周期相比，拟议的循环在热效率、具体工作和技术可行性方面具有显著优势。

具有液化天然气冷利用和燃气轮机废气源的新型跨临界二氧化碳兰金循环（[Lin*等人*，2009年](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0205)）预计效率为44%的LHV。它没有考虑到亚利桑那州立大学或燃气轮机排气（散热片）的循环。它是一种使用液化天然气作为冷水槽的新型零排放二氧化碳捕获系统。已经对一种新型的COOLCEP系统（[Zhang*等人*，2010年](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0340)）进行了建模，其中包括一个类似二氧化碳兰金的循环，在液化天然气蒸发过程中同时产生电力和制冷。COOLCEP-S设计实现了液化天然气59%的效率。

[张和利尔（2008a）](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0330)比较了两种低排放二氧化碳系统。第一个是与蒸汽重整集成的氧燃料系统，另一个是使用单乙醇胺（MEA）吸收器/修剪器和燃料重整的发电；后一种系统旨在降低碳，然后再进入燃烧室。[张和利尔（2008年b）](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0335)在类似的操作条件下将相同的两个低排放二氧化碳系统与S-格拉兹循环进行了比较。他们俩都比S-Graz效率更高。[Nord*等人（*2009年）](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0240)探讨了NGCC发电厂燃烧前二氧化碳捕获过程的各种设计和设计外分析。

总之，上述各种动力循环试图利用二氧化碳与氧燃料燃烧产物流的直接分离。循环差异集中在主要工作流体（二氧化碳或水）的选择，以及循环如何利用从加热流体中提取工作。无论配置如何，这里讨论的两个氧燃料动力循环都使用燃烧产物作为涡轮机工作流体的一部分。因此，他们不能直接使用煤炭等固体燃料，因为煤灰或微量物种会损坏旋转的硬件。相反，必须使用气化煤或清洁燃烧产品来为循环提供动力。因此，对于固体燃料的使用，氧燃料循环的性能应特别与综合气化和联合循环（IGCC）进行比较，其中二氧化碳在燃料流中发生，导致氢燃料联合循环。[第四章](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500043.htm)详细讨论了IGCC的方法。



## 6.3 组件和性能考虑

氧燃料动力循环的性能可以从工艺流程图中估计，使用压缩机和涡轮机等各种组件的材料和能量平衡。然而，许多组件尚未在拟议循环配置所需的环境中开发或演示。如下文所述，可以采用一些现有组件进行氧气燃料操作，但在性能方面会受到相关处罚。以下各节概述了氧燃料燃烧器、涡轮机和热交换器的现状。



### 6.3.1 氧气燃料循环燃烧器

上面描述的各种系统配置都需要在高压下燃烧氧气燃料。氧燃料燃烧在加工过程中很熟悉，那里的材料加工需要高温（[Baukal，1998年](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0035)）。在氧燃料动力循环中，温度受到涡轮机材料温度限制的限制。根据循环，将CO2、H2O或两者的某种组合添加到燃烧室中以控制温度。在使用氧燃料燃烧的煤粉锅炉中，回收流也可以是二氧化碳和水蒸气的一些混合物。关于煤炭应用的稀释氧燃料燃烧进行了大量研究，这些研究考虑了回收烟气如何影响煤粉的氧燃料燃烧过程（[Wall*等人*，2009年](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0305)）。

对于氧燃料动力循环，燃烧过程与煤粉甚至与现有的氧燃料工业工艺非常不同。除了峰值温度（工业与动力循环）或燃料类型（气态与固体煤）的差异外，所有动力循环应用都依赖于*加压*燃烧，如燃气轮机系统。这种区别使燃烧器的发展复杂化，只是因为具有代表性的测试需要在实验室环境中难以复制的加压条件下进行研究。

考虑到二氧化碳稀释燃烧的结果，虽然CO2、H2O和N2是这里的主要平衡产物，但其他次要物种也是可能的，必须加以考虑。不出所料，总体趋势是，平衡CO水平随着火焰温度的升高而增加，因为二氧化碳在较高温度下不太稳定，解离产生更高的CO水平。此外，对于给定的绝热火焰温度和压力，较低等效比的平衡CO水平较低，因为更高的O2水平迫使平衡向CO2而不是CO。同样，将压力从10巴增加到300巴的影响是，对于φ = 1，平衡CO降低约2.5至3倍。对于φ = 0.9，CO的还原系数在1450 K时约为5.5倍，在2050 K时为4.5倍。虽然在所附数字中没有显示，但平衡H2在产品成分中通常不显著，约占所审查病例计算CO水平的8-12%。然而，这些数字确实表明，虽然在化学计量情况下，OH自由基的平衡浓度仅为CO的6-14%，但在精益情况下，OH可以超过CO一个数量级（φ = 0.9），特别是在较低的火焰温度下。然而，请注意，在这些条件下，CO和OH的浓度都很低。例如，对于φ = 0.9和Tad![肖像](https://learning.oreilly.com/api/v2/epubs/urn:orm:book:9780857090133/files/images/x2243.gif)1450 K，我们发现CO < 10 ppm和OH ~ 20 ppm或更少。

接下来关注H2O稀释的案例（下图），水蒸气的存在意味着H2和OH将比CO2循环情况发挥更重要的作用。对于化学计量情况（φ = 1）和燃料精益情况（φ = 0.9），CO水平表现出与可比CO2循环情况相似的定性行为。然而，与可比压力、温度和等效比的二氧化碳循环情况相比，水循环情况下的一氧化碳浓度大约小5-10倍。

如上所述，最大的区别是与CO相比，H2和OH水平相对较高。对于化学计量条件，随着火焰温度从1450 K上升到2050 K，[OH]/[CO]从1.5到1.0不等。同样，在相同的温度范围内，[H2]/[CO]的比例也从约2增加到9。这些物种在较高温度下的典型浓度（量）为几千ppm，而H2的浓度为10-50 ppm，较低温度的OH和CO的浓度为10 ppm或以下。

对于φ = 1.0，将压力从10巴增加到300巴的影响是在所研究的火焰温度范围内降低约2.6至3的CO和H2。对于φ = 0.9，CO和H2的下降，压力从4.9到5.5不等。

[图](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#f0035)中的值[。6.6](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#f0035)代表完美混合和足够的停留时间来接近平衡的成分。真正的燃烧器无法实现完美的混合，并且停留时间受到燃烧室设计的实际尺寸限制的限制。化学动力学、混合设计和操作条件的组合决定了燃烧室的实际性能。与传统的空气基燃烧相比，可用于指导潜在设计的氧燃料研究相对较少，但概述如下。

对于二氧化碳稀释的火焰，[Heil*等人*（2010年）](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0135)对二氧化碳中空气与氧气的“无火焰”甲烷燃烧进行了研究。无火焰燃烧制度是在加热反应堆容器中创建的，即使在不同水平的稀释剂和不同的燃料/空气比下，燃烧温度也保持不变。由于输入流和燃烧产物之间的强烈反混，无火焰模式模仿了完美搅拌反应堆的某些方面，并提供了化学反应速率的有用指示（[Mancini*等人*，2007年](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0210)）。作者表明，二氧化碳稀释的火焰将燃料转化为一氧化碳以及二氧化碳氧化为二氧化碳的转换明显不同。此外，在空气燃烧情况下，将空气中的输入氧浓度从18%更改为21%，对CO的生产几乎没有影响。相比之下，在氧燃料的情况下，将氧气从18%改为21%（以二氧化碳为稀释剂）将反应堆核心的二氧化碳水平降低了两倍。同样，这些研究是在等温反应器中进行的——结果没有因温度与氧气水平的变化而混淆。这意味着动感速率对氧燃料的氧气水平非常敏感，但对常规空气燃烧不敏感。这些研究表明，二氧化碳不仅仅是一种热稀释剂，而是在化学上参与反应，往往会抑制二氧化碳转化为二氧化碳。

[Kutne*等人（*2011年）](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0200)使用实用的燃烧室几何学研究了二氧化碳稀释的氧燃料燃烧。燃油喷射器基于液体燃料喷气设计，其中氧化流注入环绕环形燃料片（CH4）的旋转环形环和核心。之前的空气/CH4燃烧研究也使用了相同的喷射器设计。对层流火焰速度的测量表明，当氧气水平增加30-40%时，火焰速度会增加2.4倍。火焰速度与氧气水平的这种显著敏感性突出表明，在设计全尺寸组件时，需要了解氧燃料系统的基本物理和化学。

[Chorpening*等人*](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0080)首次报告了为氧燃料再加热循环设计的燃烧器的演示测试[*。*（2005年](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0080)）。这些作者在使用蒸汽稀释的氧燃料系统再加热阶段所需的条件下，使用蒸汽稀释测试了1兆瓦的再热燃烧器。燃烧器使用水冷衬里和扩散火焰喷射器。蒸汽和氧气在燃烧室的上游混合，其设计类似于传统燃气轮机中的燃料/空气预混器。在高达10个大气压力下的测试结果将CO水平与平衡预测的水平进行了比较。假设燃烧衬里的特定热损失，测量的CO水平与平衡预测的CO水平相当。从燃烧室壁到冷却水的热损失在燃烧温度中起着明显的作用，这反过来又对平衡有很大影响（见[图）。6.6](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#f0035)）。由于一氧化碳对实际火焰温度的敏感性，作者指出，在燃烧室效率与计划燃烧温度和多余氧气的设计估计中应考虑热损失。

最近，[Anderson*等人*（2010年）](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0030)报告了基本上全尺寸单罐再热燃烧器的测试。该组件设计直接改编自商用J79/LM1500燃气轮机燃烧室，进行了修改，以提供燃烧室入口上游的蒸汽/氧气混合，并对衬里流孔进行一些更改。燃烧衬里的冷却方式与传统的风冷设计相同，蒸汽流过燃烧室壁的冷却裙。测试的条件与计划的氧燃料动力循环相当：313°C入口，压力高达8.2巴。测试结果表明，在这种实用设计中可以实现稳定的点火和燃烧，但还需要额外的改进才能实现高效、稳定的燃烧。稳定燃烧所需的氧/CH4比为7.0，而所需的化学计量值为4.0。对数据的分析表明，燃烧室空气动力学产生了非常丰富的核心火焰，导致效率和稳定性低下，应通过改进燃烧室前部的混合设计来解决这个问题。

氧燃料循环燃烧器的讨论总结如下。与气动动力循环相比，燃烧系统预计将具有不同的要求和性能——特别是因为燃烧器被故意设计为在可变稀释剂的化学计量条件下运行。将水用作稀释剂将对干式燃烧产物和湿式燃烧产物的“小”物种浓度产生重大影响。稀释剂（二氧化碳或水）以目前正在探索的方式影响燃烧化学，需要充分理解，才能为这些系统开发实用的燃烧器设计。用于再加热应用的氧燃料燃烧系统的测试表明，热损耗和空气动力学混合在燃烧性能中起着重要作用，应在拟议设计中进行仔细分析。



### 6.3.2 氧气燃料涡轮膨胀器

再次参考[表6.1](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#t0010)，许多有前途的氧燃料系统依靠高温涡轮机膨胀来发电。许多系统都配备了1300°C（2370°F）涡轮机——这是使用空气的固定燃气轮机的典型温度。即使在传统的风力涡轮机中，这种温度也可能需要叶片冷却。固定式燃气轮叶片中使用的先进合金将允许通常约为860°C（1580°F）的金属温度（[Jonsson*等人*，2005年](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0180)；[Ogata，2010年](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0245)）。刀片冷却和隔热涂层（TBC）的组合用于保护金属免受高温（~1300°C）工作流体的影响。在使用薄膜冷却的地方，冷却流由压缩机排放物提供，通过刀片或定子内部，并通过刀片表面的薄膜冷却孔注入。注入的气体产生一层冷却剂膜，保护底层叶片。

注入冷却流对于允许在高效发动机运行所需的温度下运行至关重要。然而，过多的冷却流会降低发动机效率，因为它降低了工作流体的焓，并产生压力损失，即注入的液体加速到涡轮机通道的流量速度。因此，在薄膜冷却实现的燃烧温度的效率收益与注入冷却流相关的效率损失之间存在权衡。在常规燃气轮机系统中，[Horlock*等人*（2001年*）*](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0140)使用具有当代燃气轮机典型参数的冷却模型考虑了冷却流对传统燃气轮机性能的影响。假设具有代表性（最大）叶片金属温度为800°C，作者计算了冷却外壳和相应情况下不冷却之间的发动机效率差异。在典型的发动机发射温度和压力下，效率差异约为2%。这个例子强调了冷却流对系统效率的潜在重大影响，在评估拟议的高级循环时应该认识到这一点。

[Jonsson*等人*（2005年）](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0180)的一篇有趣的论文将传统“空气燃烧”燃气轮机（Air-GT）的冷却流量和涡轮机性能与SCOC-GT进行了比较。该研究的目的不是优化这两个系统的联合循环性能，而是专注于开发一个可用于分析组合循环配置的涡轮冷却模型。对冷却模型和相关效率预测进行了“调整”，以匹配用于预测发动机性能的商业分析工具。作者指出，Air-GT模型的预测与商用燃气轮机的（专有）性能相匹配。经过此调整，只需将流体特性更改为拟议的二氧化碳系统，就可以预测SCOC-GT的行为。

虽然Air-GT和SCOC-GT循环可能会在不同的压力比下最佳运行，但在常见的涡轮入口条件下考虑[Jonsson*等人*（2005年）](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0180)的结果是有益的。例如，如果两个循环的涡轮压力比为17，燃烧室出口温度为1425°C，作者计算了将叶片温度保持在860°C以下所需的冷却流量。他们的分析报告称，Air-GT的冷却流量为22%，SCOC-GT的冷却流量为20.5%。这些可观的流量再次强调需要将冷却流要求纳入对高级循环的任何分析中。同样有趣的是，本研究中报告的涡轮机效率约为86%，略低于大多数分析中通常使用的情况。同样，对于Air-GT案例，作者报告称，这些值已“调整”到具有代表性的燃气轮机性能。需要注意的是，效率和冷却流量分数的实际值通常是给定涡轮机供应商的专有值，因此很难评估预测组件性能的准确性。[Chiesa和Macchi（2004）](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0075)报告了几种配置（包括闭环系统）冷却流的名义值；其中一些可以与[Jonsson*等人*](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0180)的Air-GT假设进行比较[*。*（2005年](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0180)）。

[Jordal*等人*](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0185)对氧燃料循环的叶片冷却进行了另一项研究[*。*（2004年](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0185)）。这些作者考虑了三种不同复杂性的模型，以评估比较氧燃料稳态性能和动态行为所需的细节水平。作者使用更简单的两个模型来评估温度和压力对Air-GT与SCOC-GT的热效率和冷却流量的影响。这两个周期以及使用任一模型方法时，行为在质量上都相似。恒压比的效率随着燃烧温度的降低而略有下降。作者认为，这可能是由于较高温度所需的更高冷却流量的影响，以及分析中使用的固定金属温度（850°C）。根据型号和条件，所需的冷却流量约为设计点条件下压缩机放电的19-20%。

在另一项对高级循环叶片冷却的研究中，[Fiaschi*等人（*2009年）](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0110)考虑了MATIANT循环的简化版本。流程配置与SCOC-CC非常相似。浇头循环使用二氧化碳作为工作流体，峰值压力为110巴，燃烧室出口温度为1300°C。涡轮机膨胀分两个阶段进行，由出口温度为1000°C的再热燃烧室隔开。浇头循环的废气提供HRSG和蒸汽底层循环所需的热能。

这项研究的独特之处在于，作者明确评估了叶片冷却对循环效率的影响。正如[Jonsson和Bolland（2005年）](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0180)所讨论的那样，[Fiaschi*等人（*2009年）](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0110)认识到薄膜冷却的惩罚，提议在两个涡轮机阶段对喷嘴和叶片进行闭环蒸汽冷却。今天最先进的固定燃气轮机目前在固定的第一级喷嘴和燃烧室过渡件上使用闭环蒸汽冷却，但在旋转设备中则不使用（[Matta*等人*，2000年](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0230)）。这种方法的好处是显著的，因为它与薄膜冷却相比，它减少了第一阶段喷嘴的工作流体温度下降。除了控制金属温度外，闭环蒸汽基本上还恢复了一些热量，否则这些热量会通过常规薄膜冷却注入降解。[Fiaschi*等人（*2009年）](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0110)将效率与这个先进的冷却概念与不使用冷却的假设情况进行了比较。如果不冷却，LHV效率为55.5%，而包括叶片冷却在内的54.1%——下降了1.4%。与未冷却外壳相比，叶片冷却将第一阶段膨胀的出口温度降低了约150K。这些结果强调了将现实的冷却流纳入对零排放循环拟议概念的重要影响。

一系列相关论文（[Hammer*等人*，2009年](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0130)；[Rezvani*等人*，2009年](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0260)；[Woollatt和Franco，2009年](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0315)）考虑了S-Graz周期和SCOC-CC的组成部分问题和经济评估。从涡轮机空气动力学设计开始，[Woollatt和Franco（2009年）](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0315)考虑了SCOC-CC的压缩机设计参数。由于工作流体大多是二氧化碳，与空气呼吸气体涡轮机中使用的传统轴流压缩机相比，较低的声音速度（与空气相比）要求压缩机以更低的速度或直径更小。在不改变空气压缩机的几何形状的情况下，在某些情况下，效率预计将比常规操作低4%。虽然可以构建一个全新的设计，但作者还预测了具有不同刀片、不同工作速度和质量流量的压缩机的性能。

从上面的讨论中可以清楚地看出，最高效率肯定需要专门为容纳二氧化碳/蒸汽工作流体而设计的涡轮机。认识到专用涡轮机的巨大开发成本，[Anderson*等人*（2008年](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0020)、[2009年）](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0025)讨论了开发适合其拟议氧气燃料系统的三代涡轮机技术的战略。在他们的周期中（见[图6.3](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#f0020)），主要工作流体是蒸汽，二氧化碳水平按体积计算低于9%。根据系统研究，Anderson*等人*指出，中等压力涡轮机（IPT）温度对热效率的影响最大。因此，这三代技术由该温度划分，[表6.3](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#t0020)总结了每代技术的关键属性。如前所述，前两代是对现有涡轮机的改装，为开发一些初始氧燃料应用提供了低风险方法。低效率，特别是在第一代系统中，可能会被二氧化碳在增强石油回收（EOR）操作中的价值所抵消。

Anderson*等人*描述了采用涡轮机适应这些条件所需的变化。由于第一代温度低，现有的J79涡轮机主要采用，主要变化是拆除压缩机，并与氧燃料燃烧器连接。对于第二代系统，IPT叶片冷却流由离开HPT的蒸汽提供，在再加热燃烧室之前提取。冷却气体将遵循类似于传统Air-GT中冷却空气的路径。空气涡轮机与氧气燃料涡轮机之间的一些具体区别如下：

•由于工作流体特性不同，氧燃料与空气相比，每个涡轮机级的温度降幅较小。结果是，第三台涡轮机级将升温约100°C，可能需要叶片冷却或新材料。

•通过冷却叶片的热通量将随着氧燃料而更高，即使在相同的金属峰值温度（例如更高的梯度）下也会带来更高的热应力。

•涡轮级的气体速度将随着不同的转弯角度而增加，需要潜在的叶片重新设计以达到峰值效率。

氧气燃料改装的另一个重要考虑因素是叶片材料的耐用性。[Anderson*等人*（2010年）](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0030)报告了暴露在空气与氧燃料涡轮机条件下的金属优惠券测试的初步研究。虽然研究尚未完成，但所有测试合金的初步结果表明，蒸汽/二氧化碳环境比相应的Air-GT环境产生更多的氧化。有人指出，更高的水蒸气压力会改变保护氧化物刻度的形式，并加速表明热屏障键涂层稳定性的材料相的耗尽。这些实验室观测对实际刀片寿命的影响是当前研究的主题。

拟议的第三代涡轮机入口温度为1760°C。与当今一些最先进的固定燃气轮机相比，这增加了300°C（[Matta*等人）。*2000年](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0230)）。[表6.1](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#t0010)中列出的其他一些拟议循环也需要入口温度非常高的涡轮机。[Chyu*等人*（2009a](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0085)，[2009b）](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0090)研究了在这些极端条件下运行的涡轮叶片所需的一些先进的冷却和热保护。这些作者使用具有代表性的涡轮机翼型几何形状，计算了暴露在氢、合成气或（蒸汽）氧燃料环境中运行的涡轮机预期条件下的叶片的外部传热量。对于固定涡轮进气温度（1430°C）和恒定叶片表面温度，作者表明，在蒸汽氧燃料的情况下，外部传热量高出50-60%。这一结果是由于蒸汽的导热性高于传统的空气工作流体。因此，即使在可比的叶片表面温度下，氧燃料的热通量也会更大。[Chyu*等人*（2009a](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0085)，[2009b）](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0090)还研究了先进的“皮肤”冷却翼型的好处，使用叶片表面附近的冷却通道，并考虑了TBC厚度的变化。根据模型结果，需要进一步提高冷却或TBC性能，以便在拟议用于第三代氧燃料应用的高温下运行翼型。研究人员从20世纪80年代就探索了使用液体或蒸汽冷却的新型冷却方案，他们提出了在高温下运行的可能性（[Caruvana*等人*，1980年](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0055)；[Horner，1980年](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0145)；[Kizuka*等人*，1998年](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0190)；[Okamura*等人*，2000年](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0250)）。

本节概述了在开发用于氧气燃料应用的涡轮机时需要考虑的许多要点。虽然改装电流涡轮机可能会受到一些效率的惩罚，但最高效率需要开发专门为应用设计的组件。系统研究应该认识到需要考虑冷却流量，根据上述文献，冷却流量可以将循环效率改变1-2%。氧燃料工作流体中材料的耐用性是当前研究的主题，在一些高级循环的拟议高温下运行将需要在冷却和热保护方面取得进展。



## 6.4 煤炭应用的循环运行和前景

大多数对氧燃料动力循环的分析只考虑了稳态运行和效率。在实际发电厂运行中，零件负荷和动态运行也是一个重要的考虑因素。由于本章中讨论的周期与组合周期具有共同的特点，因此预计动态操作将与NGCC工厂相似，但有几个例外。

所有氧燃料循环都需要亚利桑那州立大学，该亚利桑那州立大学必须包含在工厂动力学和控制中。虽然NGCC发电厂不熟悉这一点，但[第4章](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500043.htm)中描述的IGCC发电厂的氧气厂运营已经过精心开发。

与NGCC工厂相比，另一个区别是启动过程中存在液态水。由于氧燃料循环从燃烧中产生蒸汽或二氧化碳工作流体，因此可以预计启动操作会因需要自行产生工作流体而变得复杂。事实上，在氧燃料燃烧器的测试中，[Chorpening*等人*（2005年）](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0080)评论了在达到防止液态水凝结所需的稳态温度之前与氧燃料燃烧器启动相关的一些困难。这些并发症并没有阻止燃烧室本身的成功运行，在[Anderson和Bischoff（2006年](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0015)）描述的唯一氧气燃料动力循环电网连接测试中也没有困难。这些作者报告了他们的氧气燃料燃烧器和涡轮机的300多次启动和1000小时的运行时间。在演示的某些部分，这个逼真的氧燃料发电厂发电量超过2兆瓦。这些结果表明，动态控制、启动和关闭可以通过先进氧燃料循环的传统设计实践来解决。

在结束本章之前，讨论氧气燃料动力循环对煤炭燃料的潜力是有益的。正如导言中所指出的，关于氧燃料动力循环的大部分研究都集中在天然气上，因为燃烧产物也是涡轮加工工作流体。在涡轮机中使用时，工作流体必须不含未经处理的燃煤产品中存在的灰烬或腐蚀性气相杂质。可以使用带有热气净化的加压流化床设计来生产足够清洁的燃煤产品（[Burkett，2006年](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0040)），但更常见的建议是首先将煤炭气化，生产在动力循环中使用前经过净化的合成气体。在一系列论文中，[Chiesa和Consonni（1999a）](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0060)以及[Chiesa和Lozza（1999b](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0065)，[1999c）](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0070)比较了使用煤炭合成气体与二氧化碳捕获的三种不同选择。这三种方法如下：

1。使用水气移位与合成气体来制造氢气，并在燃烧前分离二氧化碳。

2。回收一部分IGCC废气（以增加烟气二氧化碳浓度），并使用传统的烟气洗涤器去除二氧化碳。

3。向SCOC-CC提供合成器，类似于本章中讨论的。

对于高水平的二氧化碳捕获，这三个不同选项的计算效率没有明显差异，因此可能需要根据效率以外的因素来选择技术开发。

在另一项煤合成气分析中，[Sanz*等人*（2010年）](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0285)描述了格拉茨循环与煤气化相结合的热力学和经济性能。作者将他们的结果与IGCC系统进行了比较，该系统使用水气位移从合成气中产生氢气，并使用加压胺洗涤来去除二氧化碳。作者指出，他们计算的效率异常高，因为他们忽视了一些损失，特别是合成气净化的损失。尽管如此，在同一基础上对周期配置的比较表明，格拉茨周期的效率远高于捕获的IGCC。这一结论取决于实现假设的周期性能，但须遵守本章中描述的许多发展问题。[Pronske*等人*（2006年）](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0255)对带有水回收的煤炭合成气氧燃料发电系统进行了初步分析，并表明与传统的IGCC相比，效率是可能的。更详细的分析，并证明组件性能，可能会受益于目前正在进行的天然气燃料系统开发研究中获得的数据和经验。



## 6.5 结论

氧气燃料动力循环被认为是二氧化碳固存发电的高效方法。由于氧燃料燃烧产物只是二氧化碳和水，所以二氧化碳分离是通过简单地凝结废气流来实现的。文献中提出了不同的循环配置，这在很大程度上取决于二氧化碳还是水被回收来控制氧燃料燃烧温度。

本章回顾了一些新的氧燃料循环配置，并描述了实现高效率所需的组件运行条件/性能。这些循环中的燃烧系统与传统的NGCC大不相同，因为氧气燃料工艺设计得理想，在没有多余的氧气的情况下运行。在实践中，需要一些多余的氧气才能很好地燃烧。任何“未使用的”氧气和部分燃烧产物（即一氧化碳）都与二氧化碳一起出现在废气中。如本章所述，在主要使用蒸汽作为工作流体的循环在主要水蒸气从废气中凝结后，可能会有相对较大的多余氧气（10-15%）。这一特点强调需要开发能够高效运行的燃烧器，而多余的氧气很少。

为了提高效率，大多数循环依赖高温涡轮机膨胀来从工作流体中提取电力。与现有的燃气轮机相比，工作流体的变化可能需要专门制造的组件来达到所需的效率。循环效率的计算需要考虑在等于或高于现有NGCC发电厂（即超过1400°C）的温度下运行的涡轮机组件可能需要的潜在大量冷却流量。最近才报告了对高蒸汽或二氧化碳工作流体中涡轮机材料耐久性的研究，这表明粘结涂层材料在蒸汽/氧燃料环境中加速降解，但在这个问题上还需要做更多工作。关于先进冷却方案的一些工作表明，在效率非常高（即高于1700°C）的温度下运行，需要进一步推进冷却技术。

为了降低开发专用氧气燃料涡轮机系统的重大技术风险，目前至少有一家开发商正在实施多代性能战略。在第一代中，几乎是商业化的组件正被用于具有公认性能限制的示范项目。迄今为止，演示测试已经显示了在网格连接的应用程序中合适的动态性能。

总之，截至本文撰写时，氧气燃料系统的开发正在进行中。研究和演示项目表明，这些循环的许多方面与当前技术相似，但必须专门为氧气燃料应用开发，以实现尽可能高的效率。本章概述了部署这些创新动力循环必须解决的一些主要技术问题。

# Novel cycles: oxy-combustion turbine cycle systems

G. Richards,   National Energy Technology Laboratory, USA

M. Williams,   URS Corporation, USA

K. Casleton,   National Energy Technology Laboratory, USA

## Abstract:

This chapter presents a discussion of recent developments in oxy-fuel combustion systems for the creation of near-zero emission power plants to control CO2 emissions. Rather than oxy-fuel approaches for pulverized coal boilers, the focus in this review is on oxy-fuel turbine-based combined cycle systems. These power cycles seek to capitalize on the direct separation of CO2 from an oxy-fuel combustion product stream as a method to manage CO2 emissions during power generation. This review discusses some of the novel oxy-fuel cycle configurations and describes the component operating conditions as well as performance needed to achieve high efficiency. A summary of the status of oxy-fuel combustors, turbomachinery and heat exchangers is presented; it discusses points that need to be considered in the development of turbomachinery for oxy-fuel applications. While a variety of different cycle configurations have been proposed in the literature, some of these cycle differences center on the choice of the dominant working fluid (CO2 or water) and how the cycle exploits the extraction of work from the heated fluid. Compared with existing gas turbines, the change in working fluid is likely to require specially built components to achieve desired efficiencies. Further, the combustion system in these cycles is substantially different from those in conventional natural gas combined cycles because the oxy-fuel process is ideally designed to operate with no excess oxygen. Impacts of these and other differences are discussed.

## 6.1 Introduction

During the last decade, oxy-fuel combustion has gained significant attention as a method of managing CO2 during power generation. The concept is straightforward: use oxygen rather than air for the combustion process. With the oxy-fuel approach, the ideal combustion products from any hydrocarbon are just CO2 and H2O, resulting in very simple CO2 removal, because water can be easily condensed. For pulverized coal boilers, the oxy-fuel approach is being explored in significant research ([Figueroa *et al.*, 2008](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0115)) and demonstration efforts ([Cook, 2009](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0095); [Stromberg *et al.*, 2009](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0295)) because it offers an alternative to scrubbing the flue gas of CO2 from conventional boiler designs. In practice, the development of oxy-fuel boilers is complicated by the need to supply pure oxygen, rather than air, so that careful consideration must be given to integrating large air separation units (ASUs) with the rest of the process. The energy required to supply the oxygen is significant, but advanced ASU designs are projected to reduce the energy needed for oxygen production ([Klosterman and Kather, 2010](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0195)), and careful integration of the ASU and power cycle is expected to provide further benefits to cycle efficiency ([Trainier *et al.*, 2010](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0300)).

The interest in oxy-fuel boilers is understandable given the large installed base of solid fuel power plants. Although steam power plants have been highly developed for efficiency, they are constrained by performance limits imposed by Rankine cycle configurations. As discussed elsewhere in this book, a key advantage of combined cycles is that the combination of two technologies can allow operation at higher overall efficiency than Rankine cycle configurations. The potential efficiency benefit is highly desirable as a means of offsetting the energy penalty associated with oxygen production needed for CO2 separation. This potential efficiency advantage explains the proposed development of oxy-fuel combined cycles discussed in this chapter. As will be seen, multiple process configurations have been proposed to exploit the potential for higher efficiency.

This chapter is arranged in the following manner. [Section 6.2](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#s0015) introduces the most common oxy-fuel power cycles and presents a comparison of some of the recent papers describing advanced oxy-fuel power cycles. [Section 6.3](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#s0020) describes research devoted to some of the key components that are necessary to build the oxy-fuel cycles, especially the combustor and turbine expanders needed to operate in the oxy-fuel environment. [Section 6.4](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#s0040) provides some comments on the operation of proposed cycles, and [Section 6.5](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#s0045) is a summary.



## 6.2 Oxy-fuel power cycle configurations

[Figure 6.1](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#f0010) shows a simple schematic of two major types of oxy-fuel power cycles. On the left ([Fig. 6.1a](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#f0010)), a clean hydrocarbon CxHy is burned in a high-pressure combustor with oxygen and recycled water. The injected water serves to control the peak temperature, producing steam and CO2 that can be expanded in a turbine as shown. After leaving the turbine, the gases can be further cooled so that condensed water is returned to the combustor for temperature control, while the separated CO2 can be compressed and sent to geologic storage. The cycle in [Fig. 6.1a](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#f0010)is essentially a Rankine cycle, but instead of a boiler, the steam is produced by direct combustion. Compared to a conventional Rankine cycle, the modest amount of CO2 in the steam working fluid (< 10% by volume) complicates the condensation because the noncondensing gas raises the volume flow in the condenser.

**6.1** Oxy-fuel power cycles with H2O recycle (a) and CO2 recycle (b).

[Figure 6.1b](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#f0010) shows a second oxy-fuel power cycle. The hydrocarbon is again burned at pressure with oxygen, but CO2, rather than water, is recycled to control the flame temperature. In this configuration, the working fluid is mostly CO2 with some modest level of steam (< 10% volume). The combustion products expand through a turbine to create power. The turbine exhaust is further cooled, allowing water removal and separation of the produced CO2. The remainder of the CO2 is compressed and returned to the combustor. The cycle configuration is essentially a semiclosed Brayton cycle, where compression and expansion occur in a manner directly analogous to a gas turbine, but using a different working fluid.

The cooling step in [Fig. 6.1b](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#f0010) could be accomplished with a heat recovery steam generator (HRSG), which in turn would drive a separate Rankine cycle. The concept is shown in [Fig. 6.2](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#f0015). The process looks remarkably similar to the natural gas combined cycles (NGCCs) discussed elsewhere in this book, except that the working fluid for the Brayton cycle is a mixture of CO2 and water vapor, and the oxidant is pure oxygen. These differences introduce both performance penalties and design challenges, which are discussed in this chapter. For consistency with other literature, the process in [Fig. 6.2](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#f0015) will be referred to as the semiclosed oxy-combustion combined cycle (SCOC-CC).

**6.2** Oxy-fuel power cycle with CO2 recycle and HRSG supplying heat for the Rankine cycle. This cycle is commonly referred to as the SCOC-CC.

It is also possible to develop more efficient versions of the cycle shown in [Fig. 6.1a](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#f0010) in which water vapor is the primary working fluid. The most direct modification to the cycle is the inclusion of one or more reheat stages, similar to a conventional power plant steam cycle. A process diagram including one additional reheat stage is shown in [Fig. 6.3](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#f0020). Because the working fluid is the combustion product, it is possible to reheat the fluid simply by burning more fuel and oxygen in subsequent reheat stages and continuing the expansion process. In principle, this approach can lead to efficiencies much higher than in conventional steam power cycles, because the turbine inlet temperatures are not limited by boiler tube temperature limits. Instead, combustion in the working fluid allows the temperature to be set as high as practically allowed by the turbomachinery, similar to gas turbine technology. The practical development of this cycle requires turbomachinery capable of operating with high-temperature steam, as opposed to air in gas turbines. The cycle in [Fig. 6.3](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#f0020) is not a true combined cycle but it is included in this discussion because it is closely related to the zero-emission concepts described in this chapter. The concept has been extensively developed by commercial entrepreneurs and is documented in a series of papers ([Hustad *et al.*, 2005](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0150); [Pronske *et al.*, 2006](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0255); [Anderson *et al.*, 2008](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0020), [2009](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0025), [2010/\](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0030)).

Another advanced oxy-fuel cycle is shown in [Fig. 6.4](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#f0025). The concept has also been referred to as the MATIANT cycle in a contraction of the names of those who proposed the concept ([Yantovski and Mathieu, 1996](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0320); [Mathieu and Nihart, 1999b](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0225)). This cycle uses supercritical CO2 as the working fluid and shares features with Rankine cycles because the CO2 is condensed to a liquid state for the low-temperature heat rejection. Unlike conventional steam cycles, the subsequent phase change back to a gaseous state is accomplished by a regenerative heat exchanger – this is a modest analogy to feedwater preheating in a standard steam Rankine cycle. As in other concepts, heat is added to the recycled CO2 at the high-pressure point in the cycle which was proposed to be 200 bars – similar to steam power cycle pressure.

[Figure 6.5](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#f0030) presents yet another oxy-fuel cycle configuration, typically referred to as the Graz cycle. This power cycle uses a mixture of CO2 and water vapor as the working fluid and has been extensively analyzed with several improvements suggested in a series of papers ([Jericha and Fesharaki, 1995](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0155); [Jericha and Göttlich, 2002](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0160); [Jericha *et al.*, 2004, 2006, 2008](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0165); [Sanz *et al.*, 2005a](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0170), [2005b](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0175), [2007](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0270), [2010](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0275)). The novelty of the cycle shown in [Fig. 6.5](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#f0030) is that the exhaust heat from the topping Brayton cycle high-temperature turbine is used to generate steam in the HRSG. This generated steam is expanded in a high-pressure turbine (HPT) and is subsequently used to dilute the oxy-fuel combustion in the Brayton cycle. A majority of the gas leaving the HRSG is recompressed and returned to the combustor – this is the main working fluid. While steam recompression is a novel proposal, it has the potential efficiency advantage of reducing heat rejection from the cycle via the heat of condensation. In [Fig. 6.5](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#f0030), the working fluid that is not returned to the compressor is further expanded through a low-pressure turbine before water is condensed, and the CO2 product is compressed for storage. The configuration of this low-pressure turbine and condenser is different from a conventional steam cycle because of the presence of noncondensable gas (CO2). The noncondensable gas affects the heat transfer and vacuum pump configuration. To minimize these complications, some versions of the Graz cycle have been proposed using a closed-loop, low-pressure bottoming cycle ([Jericha *et al.*, 2006](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0170)), where the heat for the low-pressure steam is supplied from an HRSG. As with other oxy-fuel concepts, the goal of various literature analyses has been to improve efficiency while retaining the simple design and CO2 separation, so that different versions of the Graz cycle have been proposed with different levels of CO2 and H2O recycle arrangements, ranging from 33% steam to 75% steam to optimize cycle efficiency. Details of components needed for steam/CO2 turbomachinery are described in [Section 6.3](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#s0020).

The standard MATIANT gas cycle ([Mathieu and Nihart, 1999a](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0220)) has a supercritical part combined with a regenerative CO2 Brayton cycle with reheat. The combustion takes place along an isobar. The fuel is injected at pressure in the burners with the mixture of the working fluid CO2 and the O2. At the exit of the combustion chamber (1300°C), the mixture CO2/H2O is reheated up to temperature in a second combustion chamber into which other fractions of fuel and O2 are injected and then expanded. In the MATIANT concept, the removed CO2 is liquid or supercritical and at high pressure. It can be used in applications where pressurized CO2is required.

The MATIANT cycle with blade cooling proposed by [Fiaschi *et al.* (2009)](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0110) has as a topping cycle a supercritical Brayton cycle with reheat and as a bottoming cycle a steam bottoming cycle with reheat (blade cooling). Gas turbine blade cooling cannot be carried out using a fraction of the compressor CO2 flow rate, because it would be technically complicated to inject a fluid like CO2 in blade channels at such high pressures and temperatures. The efficiency of a CO2 GT is more than 5% lower than that of a similar ‘air-fired’ gas turbine (air-GT), which is why the authors implemented a closed-loop steam blade cooling in their system design.

The E-MATIANT cycle ([Mathieu, 2004](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0215)) uses an oxy-fuel mixture that includes fuel, O2 produced in an ASU and CO2 as the working fluid and thermal moderator. CO2 is recycled. The combustion products are mainly H2O and CO2 with some O2. H2O is removed by condensation and the excess CO2, generated in the combustion, is removed in a supercritical state. The combustion is carried out at 1300°C. In this cycle, there is a higher turbine expander exhaust temperature (900–1000°C) than in a standard turbine operating on air (550°C).

The hybrid SOFC/E-MATIANT cycle ([Mathieu, 2004](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0215)) integrates the high-temperature SOFC with the E-MATIANT cycle described above. There is excellent temperature matching in the system. This is a more efficient powerful hybrid with efficiencies on NG of around 47–48%.

It is believed by some that the MATIANT cycle ([Mathieu and Nihart, 1999b](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0225)) needs few newly designed components, only the CO2/H2O separator. It has a wide variety of possible applications including electricity and cogeneration, pressurized fluidized bed combustion (PFBC), magnetohy-drodynamics (MHD) replacing the LP expander, coproduction of dry ice, hydrogen production and chemical looping combustion using metal oxides instead of ASU.

A liquefied natural gas (LNG) quasi-combined supercritical CO2 Rankine cycle and CO2 Brayton cycle interconnected with recuperation ([Zhang and Lior, 2006](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0325)) could result in a 65–68% efficiency on LNG. The system produces power, evaporates the LNG for further use while preventing more than 50% of the LNG exergy from going to waste during its evaporation, and produces liquefied CO2 and water as the combustion products and liquid nitrogen and argon as the ASU products.

An NGCC modified into O2/CO2 cycle has a projected lower heating value (LHV) of 51.3% ([Amann *et al.*, 2009](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0010)). These results have been compared with a conventional amine scrubbing applied to an NGCC with post-combustion capture with amine scrubbing. With a lean CO2 loading of 0.16 mol CO2/mol amine, this process leads to a net electrical efficiency of 49.1% (LHV). The conversion into an O2/CO2cycle seems to be more efficient.

A novel power cycle system composed of chemical recuperative cycle with CO2–NG (natural gas) reforming and an ammonia absorption refrigeration cycle has been proposed ([Cao and Zheng, 2006](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0045), [2007](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0050)).

Two cases of an advanced oxy-fuel power cycle offering 50% efficiency have been developed ([Gou *et al.*, 2006](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0125)). Case 1 basically consists of a water-steam Rankine cycle and a steam–CO2 recuperative–reheat cycle. Case 2 integrates some characteristics of Case 1 and a topping Brayton cycle. The MATIANT and CES cycles were evaluated as references under common assumptions. The proposed cycle has notable advantages in thermal efficiency, specific work and technical feasibility, compared with the MATIANT and CES cycles.

A novel transcritical CO2 Rankine cycle with LNG cold utilization and with heat source from gas turbine exhaust ([Lin *et al.*, 2009](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0205)) has a projected efficiency of 44% LHV. It does not take into account the ASU or the cycle from which the gas turbine exhaust (heat sink) comes. It is a novel zero-emission CO2 capture system using an LNG as cold sink. A novel COOLCEP system ([Zhang *et al.*, 2010](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0340)) has been modeled which includes a CO2 Rankine-like cycle producing both power and refrigeration simultaneously in an LNG evaporation process. The COOLCEP-S design achieves 59% efficiency with LNG.

[Zhang and Lior (2008a)](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0330) have compared two low-emission CO2 systems. The first is an oxy-fuel system integrated with steam reforming and the other is power generation with monoethanolamine (MEA) absorber/stripper and fuel reforming; the latter system is meant to lower carbon before it goes to the combustor. [Zhang and Lior (2008b)](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0335) compared the same two low-emission CO2 systems with the S-Graz cycle under similar operating conditions. Both of them had better efficiency than the S-Graz. [Nord *et al.* (2009)](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0240) explored various design and off-design analyses of a precombustion CO2 capture process in an NGCC power plant.

In summary, the various power cycles presented above seek to capitalize on the direct separation of CO2 from an oxy-fuel combustion product stream. Cycle differences center on the choice of the dominant working fluid (CO2 or water) and how the cycle exploits the extraction of work from the heated fluid. Whatever the configuration, both the oxy-fuel power cycles discussed here use the products of combustion as part of the working fluid in turbomachinery. As such, they cannot directly use solid fuels like coal, because coal ash or trace species would damage rotating hardware. Instead, gasified coal or cleaned combustion products must be used to power the cycle. Thus, for use with solid fuel, the performance of oxy-fuel cycles should be compared especially with integrated gasification and combined cycles (IGCCs), where the CO2 removal occurs in the fuel stream, resulting in a hydrogen-fuel combined cycle. [Chapter 4](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500043.htm) discusses the IGCC approach in detail.



## 6.3 Component and performance considerations

The performance of oxy-fuel power cycles can be estimated from process flow sheets, using material and energy balances across the various components such as compressors and turbines. However, many of the components have not been developed or demonstrated in the environment desired in proposed cycle configurations. As explained below, it is possible to adopt some existing components to oxy-fuel operation, but with associated penalties in performance. In the sections that follow, a summary of the status of oxy-fuel combustors, turbomachinery, and heat exchangers is presented.



### 6.3.1 Combustors for oxy-fuel cycles

The various system configurations described above all require oxy-fuel combustion at elevated pressure. Oxy-fuel combustion is familiar in the process industry where high temperatures are desired for materials processing ([Baukal, 1998](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0035)). In the oxy-fuel power cycle, the temperature is restricted by material temperature limits for the turbomachinery. Depending on the cycle, CO2, H2O or some combination of the two is added to the combustor to control the temperature. In pulverized coal boilers using oxy-fuel combustion, the recycle stream can also be some mixture of CO2 and water vapor. There have been a significant number of studies on diluted oxy-fuel combustion for coal applications that consider how recycled flue gas affects the pulverized coal oxy-fuel combustion process ([Wall *et al.*, 2009](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0305)).

For oxy-fuel power cycles, the combustion process is very different from pulverized coal or even from existing oxy-fuel industrial processes. Apart from the difference in peak temperatures (industrial versus power cycles), or fuel type (gaseous versus solid coal), all of the power cycle applications rely on *pressurized*combustion, like gas turbine systems. This distinction complicates the development of combustors simply because representative testing requires studies at pressurized conditions that are challenging to reproduce in a lab environment.

This gas composition was obtained by considering the ideal working fluid suggested by [Sanz *et al.* (2007)](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0280) (94% CO2 and 6% H2O). For the purpose of this example, this composition was adjusted to account for assumed levels of 1% excess oxygen and 1000 ppmv CO in a real process. Referring to [Table 6.2](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#t0015), notice that the relatively small amount of water that is condensed means that the minor constituents (CO and oxygen) remain small after the water is removed. While there would still be reason to remove the oxygen, the approach may be different when treating 15% versus 1%.

The above comparisons between H2O-and CO2-diluted products are representative examples, but do not reflect equilibrium or mixing limits that are inherent with either system. The actual equilibrium depends on the combustion temperature and precise concentrations associated with a given design and operating condition. Furthermore, the actual combustion performance may not attain equilibrium values, depending on the combustor and mixing performance. To address these distinctions, we first consider how the equilibrium behaves as a function of operating conditions for a water recycle and CO2 recycle case. Similar calculations were presented by [Richards *et al.* (2005)](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0265), but are extended here to higher pressures. [Figure 6.6](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#f0035) shows equilibrium calculations for oxy-fuel combustion at conditions representative of a CO2-diluted cycle (top) and an H2O-diluted cycle (bottom). The specific inlet conditions are 527 K reactants, methane fuel, 95% O2/5% N2 with water or CO2 as a diluent, depending on the cycle configuration. Where CO2 is used as the diluent, the specific compositiosn includes some moisture and nitrogen, as would be the case in a realistic recycle loop having a composition CO2/H2O/N2 of 0.874/0.12/0.006 respectively. Calculations were carried out using the public domain equilibrium code Cantera (v. 1.7) ([Goodwin, 2003](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0120)), and the fuel air ratio was adjusted over a range to produce the data over the adiabatic temperatures shown in [Fig. 6.6](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#f0035).

Considering the result for CO2-diluted combustion, while CO2, H2O and N2 are the primary equilibrium products here, other minor species are also possible and must be considered. As expected, the general trend is that equilibrium CO levels increase with increasing flame temperature because CO2 is less stable at higher temperatures, dissociating to produce higher CO levels. Also, for a given adiabatic flame temperature and pressure, the equilibrium CO level is lower for lower equivalence ratios because of the higher O2 levels forcing the equilibrium toward CO2 rather than CO. Likewise, the effect of increasing pressure from 10 to 300 bars is to reduce the equilibrium CO by a factor of about 2.5 to 3 for *ϕ* = 1. For *ϕ* = 0.9, this reduction in CO is by a factor of about 5.5 at 1450 K and 4.5 at 2050 K. Although not shown in the accompanying figures, equilibrium H2 was typically not significant in the product composition, approximately 8–12% of the calculated CO levels for the cases examined. These figures do show, however, that while equilibrium concentration of OH radicals was only 6–14% of CO for stoichiometric cases, the OH could exceed the CO by up to an order of magnitude for the lean cases (*ϕ* = 0.9), particularly at lower flame temperatures. Note, however, that the concentrations for both CO and OH are low for these conditions. For example, for *ϕ* = 0.9 and Tad ![image](https://learning.oreilly.com/api/v2/epubs/urn:orm:book:9780857090133/files/images/x2243.gif) 1450 K, we find CO < 10 ppm and OH ~20 ppm or less.

Focusing next on the case with H2O dilution (bottom figures), the presence of water vapor means that H2 and OH will play more significant roles than in the CO2 cycle case. For the stoichiometric cases (*ϕ* = 1) and fuel lean cases (*ϕ* = 0.9), the CO levels exhibit a similar qualitative behavior as for the comparable CO2 cycle case. However, the CO concentrations in the water cycle case are approximately a factor of 5–10 smaller compared with the CO2 cycle case for comparable pressure, temperature and equivalence ratio.

As mentioned above, the biggest difference is the presence of relatively high levels of H2 and OH compared to CO. For stoichiometric conditions, [OH]/[CO] varied from about 1.5 to 1.0 as the flame temperature was increased from 1450 to 2050 K. Similarly, the ratio [H2]/[CO] also increased from about 2 to 9 over that same temperature range. Typical concentration (volumetric) for these species for the higher temperature is a few thousand ppm while the concentrations are 10–50 ppm for H2 and 10 ppm or less for OH and CO for the lower temperatures.

For *ϕ* = 1.0, the effect of increasing pressure from 10 to 300 bars was to decrease both CO and H2 by factors of about 2.6 to 3 over the range of flame temperatures that was studied. For *ϕ* = 0.9, this decrease in CO and H2 with increasing pressure ranged from 4.9 to 5.5.

The values in [Fig. 6.6](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#f0035) represent the composition that would exist with perfect mixing and with sufficient residence time to approach equilibrium. A real combustor would not achieve perfect mixing, and the residence time is limited by practical size constraints on the combustor design. A combination of chemical kinetics, mixing design and operating conditions defines the actual combustor performance. Compared with conventional air-based combustion, relatively few oxy-fuel studies are available to guide potential designs, but are summarized below.

For CO2-diluted flames, [Heil *et al.* (2010)](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0135) have conducted a study of the ‘flameless’ methane combustion with air versus oxygen in CO2. The flame-less combustion regime was created in a heated reactor vessel, allowing the combustion temperature to be held constant even for different levels of diluents and at different fuel/air ratios. Because of the intense backmixing between input stream and combustion products, the flameless mode mimics some aspects of a perfectly stirred reactor and provides a useful indication of chemical reaction rates ([Mancini *et al.*, 2007](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0210)). The authors showed that the CO2-diluted flame had significantly different conversion of fuel to CO, as well as oxidation of the CO to CO2. Furthermore, in the air combustion case, changing the input oxygen concentration in air from 18% to 21% had little effect on the production of CO. In contrast, for the oxy-fuel case, changing the oxygen from 18% to 21% (with CO2 as the diluents) reduced CO levels in the core of the reactor by a factor of two. Again, these studies were carried out in an isothermal reactor – the results were not confounded by changing temperature versus oxygen level. The implication is that kinetic rates were very sensitive to oxygen levels for oxy-fuel, but not for conventional air firing. These studies demonstrate that CO2 is not merely a thermal diluent, but instead participates chemically in the reaction, tending to inhibit the conversion of CO to CO2.

[Kutne *et al.* (2011)](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0200) have studied CO2-diluted oxy-fuel combustion using a practical combustor geometry. The fuel injector was based on a liquid-fueled airblast design, where oxidized streams are injected in swirling annulus and core that surround an annular sheet of fuel (CH4). The same injector design had been used for prior studies of air/CH4 combustion. Measurements of the laminar flame speed showed that the flame speed increases 2.4x when the oxygen level is increased 30–40%. This significant sensitivity of flame speed versus oxygen level emphasizes the need to understand the basic physics and chemistry of the oxy-fuel systems when designing full-scale components.

Demonstration tests of a combustor designed for an oxy-fuel reheat cycle were first reported by [Chorpening *et al.* (2005)](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0080). These authors tested a 1 MW reheat combustor using steam dilution at conditions needed for a reheat stage of an oxy-fuel system using steam dilution. The combustor used a water-cooled liner and a diffusion flame fuel injector. Steam and oxygen were mixed upstream of the combustion chamber in a design similar to fuel/air premixers in conventional gas turbines. Results of the tests at pressures of up to 10 atmospheres compared CO levels to those predicted by equilibrium. The measured CO levels were comparable to those predicted by equilibrium – assuming specific heat loss from the combustion liner. The heat loss from the combustor walls to the cooling water plays an obvious role in the combustion temperature, which in turn has a large effect on equilibrium (see [Fig. 6.6](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#f0035)). Because of the sensitivity of CO to actual flame temperatures, the authors note that heat losses should be accounted for in design estimates of combustor efficiency versus planned firing temperature and excess oxygen.

More recently, [Anderson *et al.* (2010)](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0030) report tests of essentially a full-scale, single-can reheat combustor. The component design was a direct adaptation of a commercial J79/LM1500 gas turbine combustor, with modifications to provide the steam/oxygen mixing upstream of the combustor inlet and some changes to the liner flow holes. The combustion liner was cooled in the same manner as the conventional air-cooled design, with steam flow through cooling skirts in the combustor walls. Tests were conducted at conditions comparable to those planned for the oxy-fuel power cycle: 313°C inlet, and pressures up to 8.2 bars. The results of the test showed that stable ignition and combustion could be achieved in this practical design, but also that additional refinements were needed to achieve high-efficiency, stable combustion. The oxygen/CH4 ratio needed for stable combustion was 7.0, versus the desired stoichiometric value of 4.0. Analysis of the data suggested that the combustor aerodynamics produced a very rich core flame that was responsible for the low efficiency and stability, and this should be addressed with an improved mixing design at the front of the combustor.

The discussion of combustors for oxy-fuel cycles is summarized as follows. Compared to air-fired power cycles, the combustion system is expected to have both different requirements and performance – especially because the combustor is deliberately designed to operate near stoichiometric conditions with a variable diluent. The use of water as a diluent will have a significant effect on the ‘minor’ species concentration for dry versus wet combustion products. The diluents (CO2or water) affect the combustion chemistry in ways that are just now being explored and will need to be fully appreciated to develop practical combustor designs for these systems. Tests of oxy-fuel combustion systems for reheat applications have shown that heat loss and aerodynamic mixing play a significant role in combustion performance and should be carefully analyzed in proposed designs.



### 6.3.2 Oxy-fuel turbine expanders

Referring again to [Table 6.1](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#t0010), many of the promising oxy-fuel systems rely on high-temperature turbine expansion to produce power. Many of the systems incorporate a 1300°C (2370°F) turbine – a temperature typical of stationary gas turbines using air. Even in conventional air-fired turbines, this temperature will likely require blade cooling. Advanced alloys used in stationary gas turbine blades will permit operating metal temperatures typically around 860°C (1580°F) ([Jonsson *et al.*, 2005](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0180); [Ogata, 2010](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0245)). A combination of blade cooling and thermal barrier coatings (TBC) is used to protect the metal from the high-temperature (~ 1300°C) working fluid. Where film cooling is used, the cooling flow is supplied from the compressor discharge, passes through the blade or stator interior and is injected through the film cooling holes on the blade surface. The injected gas produces a film of coolant that protects the underlying blade.

The injected cooling flow is essential to permit operation at temperatures needed for high-efficiency engine operation. However, excess cooling flow penalizes the engine efficiency because it reduces the enthalpy of the working fluid, and it creates a pressure loss where the injected fluid is accelerated to the flow speed in the turbine passage. Thus, a trade-off exists between efficiency gains via firing temperatures enabled by film cooling versus efficiency losses associated with injecting the cooling flow. In conventional gas turbine systems, [Horlock *et al.* (2001)](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0140)consider the effect of cooling flows on conventional gas turbine performance, using a cooling model with parameters typical of contemporary gas turbines. Assuming a representative (maximum) blade metal temperature of 800°C with cooling, the authors calculate the difference in engine efficiency between the cooled case and a corresponding case without cooling. At typical engine firing temperatures and pressures, the difference is approximately 2% points in efficiency. This example emphasizes the potentially significant effect cooling flows have on system efficiency, and should be recognized in evaluation of proposed advanced cycles.

An interesting paper by [Jonsson *et al.* (2005)](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0180) compares the cooling flows and turbine performance for a conventional ‘air-fired’ gas turbine (Air-GT) to a SCOC-GT. The study did not aim to optimize the combined cycle performance of either system, but was focused on developing a model for the turbine cooling that could be used to analyze combined cycle configurations. The cooling model and associated efficiency predictions were ‘tuned’ to match a commercial analysis tool that is used to predict engine performance. The authors note that the predictions of the Air-GT model matched the (proprietary) performance of commercial gas turbines. After this tuning, the behavior of the SCOC-GT could be predicted by simply changing the fluid properties to the proposed CO2 system.

While it is likely that the Air-GT and SCOC-GT cycles would optimally operate at different pressure ratios, it is instructive to consider the results of [Jonsson *et al.*(2005)](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0180) at a common turbine inlet condition. For example, if both cycles operate with a turbine pressure ratio of 17, and combustor exit temperature of 1425°C, the authors calculate the cooling flows needed to maintain blade temperature below 860°C. Their analysis reports a cooling flow of 22% for the Air-GT and 20.5% for the SCOC-GT. These appreciable flows again emphasize the need to include cooling flow requirements in any analysis of advanced cycles. It is also interesting that the turbine efficiencies reported in this study were around 86%, somewhat lower than is typically used in most analyses. Again, for the Air-GT case, the authors report these values were ‘tuned’ to representative gas turbine performance. It is helpful to note that the actual values of efficiency and cooling flow fraction are usually proprietary to a given turbine vendor, so it is difficult to assess the accuracy of predicted component performance. [Chiesa and Macchi (2004)](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0075) report nominal values for cooling flows in several configurations including closed-loop systems; some of these can be compared to assumptions about the Air-GT of [Jonsson *et al.* (2005)](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0180).

Another study of blade cooling for oxy-fuel cycles was conducted by [Jordal *et al.*(2004)](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0185). These authors consider three models of different complexity to evaluate the level of detail needed to compare oxy-fuel steady-state performance and also dynamic behavior. The authors used the simpler two models to evaluate the effect of temperature and pressure on thermal efficiency and cooling flow in an Air-GT versus an SCOC-GT. The behavior is qualitatively similar for both cycles and when using either model approach. The efficiency at constant pressure ratio was shown to drop very slightly with firing temperature. The authors suggest this is likely due to the effect of higher cooling flow needed for higher temperatures, and the fixed metal temperatures (850°C) used in their analysis. The required cooling flow, depending on the model and conditions, was approximately 19–20% of the compressor discharge at the design point condition.

In another investigation of blade cooling for advanced cycles, [Fiaschi *et al.*(2009)](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0110) consider a simplified version of the MATIANT cycle. The process configuration is very similar to the SCOC-CC. The topping cycle uses CO2 as a working fluid with a peak pressure of 110 bars and a combustor exit temperature of 1300°C. The turbine expansion occurs in two stages separated by a reheat combustor with an exit temperature of 1000°C. The exhaust from the topping cycle supplies the thermal energy needed for the HRSG and steam bottoming cycle.

The unique aspect of this study is that the authors explicitly evaluate the effect of blade cooling on cycle efficiency. Recognizing the penalty of film cooling, as discussed in [Jonsson and Bolland (2005)](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0180), [Fiaschi *et al.* (2009)](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0110) propose closed-loop steam cooling for nozzle and vanes in both turbine stages. The most advanced stationary gas turbines today currently use closed-loop steam cooling on the stationary first stage nozzles and combustor transition piece, but not in the rotating equipment ([Matta *et al.*, 2000](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0230)). The benefit of this approach is significant because it reduces the working fluid temperature drop in the first stage nozzle compared to film cooling. Aside from controlling the metal temperatures, the closed-loop steam essentially recuperates some of the heat that would otherwise be degraded by conventional film cooling injection. [Fiaschi *et al.* (2009)](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0110) compare the efficiency with this advanced cooling concept, versus a hypothetical case where no cooling is used. Without cooling, the LHV efficiency is 55.5% versus 54.1% including blade cooling – a drop of 1.4%. The blade cooling reduces the outlet temperature of the first stage expansion by ~150 K versus the uncooled case. These results emphasize the important effect of including realistic cooling flows on proposed concepts for zero-emission cycles.

A series of related papers ([Hammer *et al.*, 2009](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0130); [Rezvani *et al.*, 2009](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0260); [Woollatt and Franco, 2009](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0315)) considers component issues and economic evaluation of both the S-Graz cycle and SCOC-CC. Starting with the turbomachin-ery aerodynamic design, [Woollatt and Franco (2009)](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0315) consider compressor design parameters for the SCOC-CC. Because the working fluid is mostly CO2, the lower speed of sound (compared to air) requires that the compressor operate at a lower speed, or with a smaller diameter, versus conventional axial-flow compressors used in air breathing gas turbines. Without changing the geometry of the air compressors, the efficiency is projected in some situations to be 4% lower than in conventional operation. While a completely new design could be built, the authors also predict the performance of a compressor with different blading, and different operating speeds and mass flow.

From the discussion above, it is clear that the highest efficiency will certainly require turbines that are specifically designed to accommodate a CO2/steam working fluid. Recognizing the significant development cost of a purpose-built turbine, [Anderson *et al.* (2008](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0020), [2009)](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0025) discuss a strategy to develop three generations of turbine technology suitable to their proposed oxy-fuel system. In their cycle (see [Fig. 6.3](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#f0020)), the main working fluid is steam with CO2 levels less than 9% by volume. Based on system studies, Anderson *et al.* note that the thermal efficiency is most dramatically affected by the temperature of the intermediate pressure turbine (IPT). Thus, the three technology generations are delineated by this temperature, and key attributes of each are summarized in [Table 6.3](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#t0020). As indicated, the first two generations are modifications to existing turbines, providing a low-risk approach to developing some initial oxy-fuel applications. The low efficiency, particularly in the first-generation system, may be offset by the value of CO2 in enhanced oil recovery (EOR) operations.

Anderson *et al.* describe the changes needed for adopting turbines to these conditions. Because of the low-temperatures in the first generation, the existing J79 turbine is mostly adopted as is, with the main change being the removal of the compressor, and connection to an oxy-fuel combustor. For the second-generation system, the IPT blade cooling flow is supplied from the steam that exits the HPT, extracted ahead of the reheat combustor. The cooling gas will follow a path similar to the cooling air in a conventional Air-GT. Some specific differences between the air versus oxy-fuel turbine are noted below:

• Because of the different working fluid properties, the temperature drop per turbine stage is smaller with oxy-fuel versus air. The result is that the third turbine stage will be about 100°C hotter, potentially requiring blade cooling or new materials.

• Heat fluxes through the cooled blades will be higher with oxy-fuel, introducing higher thermal stress even at the same peak metal temperatures (e.g., higher gradients).

• Gas velocities in the turbine stages will increase, with different turning angles, requiring potential blade re-design for peak efficiency.

Another important consideration for the oxy-fuel retrofit is the durability of blade materials. [Anderson *et al.* (2010)](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0030) report preliminary studies of metal coupon tests exposed to air versus oxy-fuel turbine conditions. Although the study is not complete, preliminary results for all alloys tested show that the steam/CO2environment produces more oxidation than the corresponding Air-GT environment. The higher water vapor pressure was noted to change the form of protective oxide scales, as well as accelerate depletion of material phases that are indicative of thermal barrier bond-coat stability. The impact of these lab observations on actual blade lifetime is a subject of current study.

The proposed third-generation turbine inlet temperature is 1760°C. This is an increase of 300°C compared to some of the most advanced stationary gas turbines available today ([Matta *et al.* 2000](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0230)). Some of the other proposed cycles listed in [Table 6.1](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#t0010) also require turbines with very high inlet temperatures. [Chyu *et al.* (2009a](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0085), [2009b)](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0090) have investigated some of the advanced cooling and thermal protection needed for turbine blades operating at these extreme conditions. Using a representative turbine airfoil geometry, these authors calculate the external heat transfer to blades exposed to conditions expected in a turbine operating with hydrogen, syngas or a (steam) oxy-fuel environment. For a fixed turbine inlet temperature (1430°C) and a constant blade surface temperature, the authors show that the external heat transfer is 50–60% higher in the steam oxy-fuel case. This result is due to the higher thermal conductivity of steam versus conventional air working fluid. As a result, the heat flux will be larger in oxy-fuel cases, even at the comparable blade surface temperature. [Chyu *et al.* (2009a](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0085), [2009b)](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0090) also investigate the benefit of advanced ‘skin’ cooling airfoils, using cooling passages located near the blade surface, and also consider variations in the thickness of the TBC. Based on model results, further advances in cooling or TBC performance would be needed to allow airfoil operation at the high temperature proposed for the third-generation oxy-fuel application. Novel cooling schemes using liquid or steam cooling have been explored by researchers dating back to the 1980s, who suggested the potential for operation at very high temperature ([Caruvana *et al.*, 1980](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0055); [Horner, 1980](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0145); [Kizuka *et al.*, 1998](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0190); [Okamura *et al.*, 2000](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0250)).

This section has outlined many of the points that need to be considered in development of turbomachinery for oxy-fuel applications. While retrofitting current turbines is possible with some efficiency penalty, the highest efficiency will require development of components specifically designed for the application. Systems studies should recognize the need to account for cooling flows, which can change the cycle efficiency by 1–2% according to literature cited above. The durability of materials in the oxy-fuel working fluid is the subject of current research, and operation at proposed high temperatures for some advanced cycles will require advances in cooling and thermal protection.



## 6.4 Cycle operation and prospects for coal applications

Most analyses of oxy-fuel power cycles have only considered steady-state operation and efficiency. In actual power plant operation, part-load and dynamic operation is also an important consideration. Because the cycles discussed in this chapter have features in common with combined cycles, it is expected that dynamic operation will be similar to NGCC plants, with a few exceptions.

All of the oxy-fuel cycles require an ASU which must be included in the plant dynamics and control. While this is not familiar to NGCC power plants, oxygen plant operation has been carefully developed for IGCC plants described in [Chapter 4](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500043.htm).

Another difference compared to NGCC plants is the presence of liquid water during start-up. Because the oxy-fuel cycles generate the steam or CO2 working fluid from combustion, it might be expected that initiating operation would be complicated by the need to generate the working fluid itself. Indeed, in tests of an oxy-fuel combustor, [Chorpening *et al.* (2005)](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0080) comment on some of the difficulties associated with oxy-fuel combustor start-up before achieving steady-state temperatures needed to prevent condensation of liquid water. These complications did not prevent successful operation of the combustor itself and presented no difficulty in the only grid-connected test of an oxy-fuel power cycle, described by [Anderson and Bischoff (2006)](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0015). These authors report more than 300 starts and 1000 h of operation of their oxy-fuel combustor and turbine. In some portions of the demonstration, this realistic oxy-fuel power plant generated more than 2 MWe of power. These results suggest that dynamic control, start-up and shutdown can be addressed with conventional design practice in advanced oxy-fuel cycles.

Before concluding this chapter, it is useful to discuss the potential of oxy-fuel power cycles for coal fuel. As noted in the introduction, much of the research on oxy-fuel power cycles has focused on natural gas because the combustion products are also the turbomachinery working fluid. For use in a turbine, the working fluid must be free of ash or corrosive gas-phase impurities that exist in untreated coal combustion products. Sufficiently clean coal combustion products might be produced using pressurized fluidized bed designs with hot-gas purification ([Burkett, 2006](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0040)), but the more common proposal is to first gasify the coal, producing synthesis gas that is purified before use in the power cycle. In a series of papers, [Chiesa and Consonni (1999a)](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0060) and [Chiesa and Lozza (1999b](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0065), [1999c)](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0070) compared three different options for using coal synthesis gas with CO2 capture. The three approaches were the following:

\1. Use water-gas shift with synthesis gas to make hydrogen, and separate the CO2 before combustion.

\2. Recycle a portion of the IGCC exhaust gas (to increase the flue gas CO2 concentration) and remove the CO2 with a conventional flue-gas scrubber.

\3. Supply syngas to a SCOC-CC, analogous to that discussed in this chapter.

For high levels of CO2 capture, there was no appreciable difference in the calculated efficiency for these three different options, so that choice of technology development might need to be made on factors beside efficiency.

In another analysis for coal syngas, [Sanz *et al.* (2010)](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0285) describe both the thermodynamic and economic performance of the Graz cycle combined with coal gasification. The authors compare their results to an IGCC system using water-gas shift to create hydrogen from the syngas and pressurized amine scrubbing to remove the CO2. The authors note that their calculated efficiencies are unusually high, because they neglect some losses, especially losses from syngas purification. Nevertheless, a comparison among cycle configurations on the same basis showed that the efficiency for the Graz cycle was much higher than in an IGCC with capture. This conclusion depends on achieving the assumed cycle performance, subject to many of the development issues described in this chapter. [Pronske *et al.* (2006)](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/combined-cycle-systems/9780857090133/xhtml/B9780857090133500067.htm#bb0255)present a preliminary analysis for a coal syngas oxy-fuel power system with water recycle, and suggests that good efficiency is possible compared to conventional IGCC with capture. More detailed analysis, with demonstrated component performance, might benefit from data and experience gained in development studies now in progress for natural gas fueled systems.



## 6.5 Conclusion

Oxy-fuel power cycles have been proposed as a high-efficiency method of generating power with CO2 sequestration. Because the oxy-fuel combustion products are just CO2 and water, the CO2 separation is accomplished by simply condensing the exhaust stream. Different cycle configurations have been proposed in the literature, largely depending on whether CO2 or water is recycled to control the oxy-fuel combustion temperatures.

This chapter has reviewed some of the novel oxy-fuel cycle configurations and described the component operating conditions/performance needed to achieve high efficiency. The combustion system in these cycles is substantially different from that of conventional NGCCs because the oxy-fuel process is ideally designed to operate with no excess oxygen. In practice, some excess oxygen is required for good combustion. Any ‘unused’ oxygen and partial combustion products (i.e., carbon monoxide) appear in the exhaust with the CO2. As described in this chapter, the cycles using primarily steam as the working fluid can have relatively large fractions of excess oxygen (10–15%) after the dominant water vapor is condensed from the exhaust. This feature emphasizes the need to develop combustors that can operate at high efficiency with very little excess oxygen.

For high efficiency, most of the cycles rely on high-temperature turbine expansion to extract power from the working fluid. Compared to existing gas turbines, the change in working fluid is likely to require specially built components to achieve desired efficiencies. Calculations of cycle efficiencies need to account for potentially significant cooling flows that are required for turbine components operating at temperatures equal to or greater than those in existing NGCC power plants (i.e., above 1400°C). Studies of turbine material durability in the high-steam or CO2 working fluid have only recently been reported and suggest accelerated degradation of bond-coat materials in the steam/oxy-fuel environment, but more work is needed on this topic. Some work on advanced cooling schemes indicates that further advances in cooling technology are needed for operation at temperatures desired for very high efficiency (i.e., above 1700°C).

To reduce the significant technical risk of developing purpose-built oxy-fuel turbine systems, at least one developer is currently pursuing a multiple-generation performance strategy. In the first generation, nearly commercial components are being used for demonstration projects with acknowledged limits on performance. To date, demonstration tests have shown suitable dynamic performance in a grid-connected application.

In conclusion, the development of oxy-fuel systems is in progress as of this writing. Research and demonstration projects have shown that many aspects of these cycles are similar to current technology, but must be specifically developed for the oxy-fuel application to achieve the highest possible efficiency. This chapter has outlined some of the major technical issues that must be addressed to deploy these innovative power cycles.
