The Road Not Taken

Last year's Fire and Ice narrative worked so well we decided to dust off another Robert

Frost jewel to describe this year's outlook with The Road Not Taken. As described by

many literary experts, and Frost himself, the poem presents the dilemma we all face in

life that different choices lead to different outcomes, and while the road taken can be a

good one, these choices create doubt and even remorse about the road not taken - i.e.,

what if/could have been? For the year ahead, we think investors will need to be more

tactical with their views on the economy, policy, earnings and valuation . This is because

we are closer to the end of the cycle at this point, and that means the trends in these

key variables can zig and zag before the final path is clear . In other words, while

flexibility is always important to successful investing, it's critical now .

In contrast, the set-up was so poor a year ago that the trends in all of the variables

mentioned above were headed lower, in our view. Therefore, the right choice/strategy

was about managing and/or profiting from the new downtrend . After all, Fire and Ice,

the poem, is not a debate about the destination - it's the end of the world. Instead, it's

about what causes it and the path to that destination. In the case of our bear market

call, it was a combination of both Fire AND Ice - inflation AND slowing growth, a

generally toxic cocktail for stocks . As it turned out, that cocktail has been just as bad for

bonds, at least so far . However, as the Ice overtakes the Fire and inflation cools off,

we're becoming more confident that bonds should handily beat stocks in this final verse

that has yet to fully play out (Exhibit 1). That divergence can create new opportunities

and confusion about the road we are on, and why we have recently pivoted to a more

bullish tactical view. For a recent discussion of our tactical views, check out the latest

episode of our Equities Unplugged podcast .

The transition from Fire to Ice is tactically bullish for stocks ...

In the near term, we maintain our tactically bullish call as we transition from Fire to Ice,

a window of opportunity when long-term interest rates typically fall prior to the

magnitude of the slowdown being reflected in earnings estimates and the economy . This

is the classic late cycle period between the Fed's last hike and the recession. Historically,

Pauses Are Different than Cuts

However, we want to remind readers that a pause is different than a cut . While some

investors may think a cut is even better than a pause in rates, the evidence does not

bear that out . Exhibit 3 shows that when the cuts coincide with a recession, it's not good

for equities. So, while we think there is a window for stocks to run into year end as the

markets dream of a pause, a Fed that is cutting is probably a bad sign that the recession

has arrived (negative payrolls) . This is especially true given the uniqueness of this cycle -

i.e., higher than target inflation and fear of a resurgence means the Fed may pause , but

won't cut rates before a recession arrives .

So far, the tactical rally call has played out. Interestingly, prior to last week's softer than

expected CPI release, it's produced very bifurcated performance, with the Dow

Industrials and small caps dominating the Nasdaq and S&P 500 . However, that all

changed last week when bonds moved higher (yields lower) on the softer CPI, a

necessary development for our tactical call to have another leg higher.

As discussed in last week's note, lower rate volatility was the key to the first leg of this

rally which supported valuations and the more cyclical parts of the equity market

initially (Exhibit 4). But, in order to get the next leg of the tactical equity rally, we argued

rate levels would need to fall. Furthermore, this leg would be led by a catch up in

Nasdaq/long duration growth stocks (Exhibit 5) relative to the Dow Industrials and

Russell 2000 . Since the S&P 500 is a large cap growth index, it too would benefit more

than the Dow. Finally, several of the mega cap tech stocks announced meaningful cost

reduction programs, including layoffs. This is a step in the right direction from a cost

management standpoint that investors can get excited about in the near term even

though we think more draconian cuts will be required before it's done . However, in the

short term, it's just another reason why money can flow back into these long duration/

rate sensitive equities. In short, the move lower in yields last week was/is the catalyst for

even higher prices for the S&P 500, even from here . While the lower end of our prior

target for this rally (4000-4150) was achieved on Friday, we think the upper end of that

range will be reached, and we would not rule out even higher prices should 10-year UST

yields fall more precipitously - i.e., 3.25%.

Getting back to our narrative for the next 12 months, the path forward is much more

uncertain than a year ago, in our view, and likely to bring several twists and days/weeks

of remorse for investors regretting they traded it differently - i.e., The Road Not Taken.

If one were to take our S&P bear/base/bull targets (3500/3900/4200) at face value,

they might say it looks like we are expecting a generally boring year . However, nothing

could be further from the truth . In fact, we would argue the past 12 months have been

pretty boring because a bear market was so likely we simply set our defensive strategy

and stayed with it -i.e., boring can be beautiful.

That strategy worked, as evidenced by the 1900 bps of outperformance of our Fresh

Money Buy List that has had less than 40 percent turnover (Exhibit 9). In fact, even

during this recent rally, our defensively oriented portfolio of names has seen some of its

best relative performance of the year . This tells us the bear market is likely not over.

More importantly, we have fully participated in the tactical rally even with a defensively

oriented portfolio that will serve us well as the market heads lower again into 1Q. In

other words, one didn't have to completely revamp the portfolio to enjoy this rally to its

fullest, which has made it easier to play along . While growth stocks are likely to do the

best if rates fall like we expect, it should also help defensively oriented stocks relative

to the average stock even if it gives back some performance relative to the longer

duration S&P 500 and Nasdaq . Finally, our tactical call was always more about getting

out of the way with one's shorts, and letting this bear breathe before the final verse of

our original Fire and Ice narrative plays out . Therefore, we will not be changing this list in

the near term even though we now think the tactical rally has legs to at least 4150.

Forecast Update

Earnings Drive A Volatile Price Path

Forecast Summary

While our year end 2023 base case price target of 3,900 is roughly in line with where

we're currently trading, it won't be a smooth ride. In short, we expect a bust before a

boom, and it comes down to earnings. Our highest conviction view across the board is

that 2023 bottom up consensus earnings are materially too high. On that score, we

revise our '23 EPS forecast another 8% lower to $195 in the base case , a reflection of

worsening output from our leading earnings models and increased conviction that

margin pressure will be greater than appreciated . This leaves us 16% below consensus

on '23 EPS in our base case and down 11% from a year-over-year growth standpoint.

After what's left of this current tactical rally, we see the S&P 500 discounting the '23

earnings risk sometime in the first quarter of next year via a ~3,000-3,300 price trough .

We think this occurs in advance of the eventual trough in EPS, which is typical for

earnings recessions . In other words, price leads earnings and it's not typical to put a

trough multiple on trough earnings . We think that means the Q1 price low is marked by

a 13.5-15X multiple on a forward EPS number of ~$220 . While we see 2023 as a very

challenging year for earnings growth , 2024 should be the opposite-a rebound growth

year where positive ope rating leverage resumes- Le., the next boom . As such , equities

should begin to process that growth reacceleration well in advance, rebounding off a

~3,000-3,300 price trough in Q1 and finishing the year at 3,900 in our base case .

Base Case Price Target for Dec. '23: 3,900

In our 3,900 base case, the market puts a 16.lx P/E multiple on forward (2024) EPS of

$241. This outcome represents a proper earnings recession (year -over -year EPS growth

contracts by 11%). We see nominal top line growth slowing to low single digit territory

(from low teens in '22). Meanwhile, margins do the heavy lifting to the downside as cost

pressures remain stickier than slowing end demand and pricing. On that front , we see

margins contracting by ~150 bps next year, taking the net margin time series back just

below its 25-year trend line. We see the S&P 500 discounting this earnings risk

sometime in the first quarter of next year in advance of the eventual trough in EPS

which is typical for earnings recessions . While we see 2023 as a very challenging year for

earnings growth, 2024 should be the opposite-a rebound growth year where positive

operating leverage resumes . As such, equities should process that growth reacceleration

well in advance, rebounding off a ~3,000-3,300 price trough in Ql and finishing the year

at ~3,900 in our base case.

Bull Case Price Target for Dec. '23: lt,200

In our 4,200 bull case, the market puts a 16.7x P/E multiple on forward (2024) EPS of

$251. This outcome represents a disappointing EPS growth backdrop for '23 but it's more

of a muddle through (-4% year-over-year EPS growth) . The correction of cycle excesses

is less pervasive and, as a result, the magnitude of the growth rebound in 2024 is less

significant than it is in our base and bear cases . In this scenario, we see nominal top line

growth slowing to positive mid single digit territory next year. Margins compress by

~100 bps, a less severe outcome than what we see in our base and bear cases . By the

end of next year, the market is processing a healthy mid-teens EPS growth rebound in

2024, and the multiple expands to ~16.7x. Because our bull case presents the least

attractive '24 EPS growth profile as '23 is more of a muddle through scenario, it also

offers a less attractive upside price skew to our base and bear cases than we've typically

forecasted . In this scenario, we don't expect new Q1 '23 price lows (i.e., we'd expect a

retest of 3,500 but not a new low).

Bear Case Price Target for Dec. '23: 3,500

In our 3,500 bear case, the market puts a 15.3x P/E multiple on forward (2024) EPS of

Detail on Our Earnings Views

Below, in Exhibit 13 and Exhibit 14, we highlight 2 important inputs to our earnings

modeling process, both of which suggest meaningful EPS growth deceleration over the

next 12 months . Our Leading Earnings Indicator (Exhibit 13) is made up of ISM

manufacturing PMI, consumer confidence, housing starts and credit spreads. This model

features dynamic weightings whereby if the correlation of a given factor falls below a

significance threshold, it is temporarily removed from the model. In Exhibit 14, we show

our new Non-PM I Leading Earnings Model. While we rely heavily on the PMI as a key

input to our process given its strong track record of explaining both earnings and price

and its Long history, we sought to create a separate Leading earnings model that did not

include the ISM PMI in an effort to triangulate output from different approaches. This

new model possesses a similarly strong r-squared when run against actual S&P 500 EPS

growth, and points to an even more severe decline in earnings growth over the next 12

months. We systematically assessed ~10,000 monthly macro series during the process

of constructing this indicator and wound up including the series listed in the footnote

below Exhibit 14. Further, the second derivative of earnings revisions breadth tends to

Lead forward EPS growth (Exhibit 15) - a relationship that also suggests downside is

coming from an earnings growth standpoint.

One of the main sources of pushback we expect to receive around the further cuts to

earnings we are making is that it's going to be difficult for nominal earnings to contract

materially because we're likely to remain in an elevated and sticky inflation backdrop .

First off, we agree inflation is likely to remain above trend for a good portion of next

year despite a decelerating primary trend . This is a key reason why our nominal top line

growth estimates are still marginally in positive territory for next year . That said, positive

nominal top line growth does not guarantee positive nominal EPS growth as our

estimates imply. The reason is margins - we have about 150 bps of margin compression

embedded in our '23 base case estimate, which takes earnings growth materially

negative. Thinking about the areas of inflation that are likely to remain more resilient

into next year (shelter, wages, certain services) and the areas that are likely to

decelerate more acutely (goods) does not paint a constructive picture for S&P 500

margins , in our view. The index is levered to wages, in particular, on the cost side and

goods, in particular, on the end demand side.

To illustrate that we have seen material earnings declines in periods of above average

inflation, we took a look at earnings growth alongside CPI going back to the 1970s

(Exhibit 25). As the chart shows, we saw a couple of material (>10%) EPS growth

declines despite still elevated inflation . The reason that's feasible is shown in Exhibit 26.

Nominal top line growth may be positive, but if cost growth exceeds sales growth , net

income growth is generally negative . These dynamics speak to the power of operating

leverage, the reason why we're focused on PPI vs. CPI spreads and why the rate of

change on nominal wage growth vs. gdp growth matters - all dynamics we've focused

on over the last several months .

remains very weak as inflation continues to plague consumers. We have seen inventories

build and are in early stages of discounting which will lead to margin degradation .

Relative valuation for the sector remains extended especially within Autos and

Consumer Services.

Discretionary companies have seen a large inventory build which is threatening

margins. The problem with inventory is twofold - supply chains bottlenecks are clearing

while demand, especially demand for goods, is slowing. Goods consumption, on a real

basis, has been declining since 2Q21 and is now back near trend. We expect overall

demand to slow beyond the payback in overconsumption of goods as sentiment remains

weak and inflation weighs on consumers.

We believe many companies will turn to aggressive discounting to solve their inventory

problem which is likely to spark a "race to the bottom" as companies attempt to cut

prices faster than peers and move out as much inventory as possible. We are already

seeing companies offer discounts and expect this to accelerate into the Holiday Season .

This dynamic should weigh heavily on margins and fuel the earnings slowdown we are

predicting. For more on inventory please see Taking Stock of the Inventory Problem .

Consumer sentiment remains near recessionary levels. While consumer sentiment has

improved from the all time lows observed this summer it still remains in recessionary

territory (Exhibit 48). Consumer sentiment began dropping last spring and accelerated

lower as inflation got hotter . Household balance sheets remain generally healthy

though we have seen excess savings levels drop as consumers tap into these reserves .

Consumers still have the capacity to spend; however, we question whether they will

continue to have the appet ite to spend especially on discretionary items .

Cyclicals vs. Defensives: Preference for Defensives

Bottom Line: We have a preference for defensives over cyclicals as reflected by our

sector ratings, our overweights are entirely Defensive sectors while our underweights

are entirely cyclical. This is a relative call, we don't think there will be a substantial

amount of absolute upside for the group but it should hold up better than cyclical

pockets. We are late cycle which is an environment which favors defensives, and rates

may now be in the process of peaking - also a relative benefit. Cyclicals also have

significantly more room for earnings to be revised lower.

Our call on defensives is a relative one. Defensives have meaningfully outperformed

cyclicals on a relative basis year to date and all defensive sectors except Real Estate

have materially outperformed the S&P 500 (Exhibit 56). With the exception of Energy,

all of the top performing sectors have come from the defensive end of the spectrum .

We do not believe defensives will have a great run of absolute performance but they

should offer some relative protection as our call for an earnings recession would hit

cyclicals harder . Cyclical areas are particularly vulnerable to a payback in demand which

we believe will broaden out over the coming months .

The problem with inventory is twofold - supply chain bottlenecks are clearing while

demand, especially demand for goods, is slowing . Supply chains have stabilized for most

industries, and goods imports into the US have surged higher . Goods consumption, on a

demand to slow beyond the payback in overconsumption of goods as sentiment remains

weak and inflation weighs on consumers. Increasing supply and falling demand is likely

to spark discounting adding fuel to the earnings slowdown we are calling for .
